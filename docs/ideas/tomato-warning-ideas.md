# Product Ideas: Tomato Warning

## Executive Summary

Tomato Warning has a clear, evidence-backed path from branding site to product launch to regional expansion. The ideation process generated 37 ideas across 8 categories — all grounded in market research. After evaluation: 15 ideas are essential for the branding site phase (PURSUE NOW), 10 are critical for product launch, and 12 are growth-phase items. Zero ideas were rejected outright — every concept linked to validated research. The brand's core differentiators (EF-scale heat system, storm theming, charitable mission, fundraising partner channel) are each individually validated and collectively unoccupied by any competitor.

The branding site's job is simple: build three lists (retailers, nonprofits, consumers) that prove demand before the product exists. Everything on the site serves that goal.

## Ideas — Ranked by Priority

### Tier 1: Pursue Now — Branding Site Phase

These 15 items are required for the branding website. Ranked by priority score.

---

#### 1. "Stock the Storm" — Retailer Interest CTA
- **Description:** Dedicated landing page for retail buyers. Headline: "Stock the Storm." Short form (name, store, location, role), retailer mailing list opt-in, downloadable one-pager PDF with product specs, price points, and shelf placement. Professional, respects buyer's time.
- **User need:** Publix and specialty retailers are the primary distribution path. Building a retailer pipeline before product launch is essential for Year 1-2 goals.
- **Evidence:** "Publix requires demonstrated marketing budget and 'extraordinary product innovation'" (Research, Regional Dynamics). Retailer mailing list is a core pre-launch asset.
- **Effort:** Small-Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Confidence:** H — directly fulfills stated site requirement (mailing list #1)
- **Risks:** PDF requires product details that may not be final. Mitigation: use ranges ("expected MSRP: $7.99-$9.99") with "subject to change" note.
- **KPI:** 50 retailer signups before Publix pitch

---

#### 2. "Fundraise for Recovery" — Nonprofit Partner CTA
- **Description:** Dedicated nonprofit/fundraising partner page. Three clear bullets: 45-50% margin, no upfront cost, custom label option. Simple interest form. Mailing list opt-in for "Fundraising Partner Updates."
- **User need:** Coach Dave persona needs a fundraising product that is shelf-stable, margin-positive, and easy to sell. The fundraising channel is unoccupied in premium salsa.
- **Evidence:** "No premium salsa brand has built a structured fundraising partner program" (Research, Market Gaps #5). Coach Dave persona validated in full.
- **Effort:** Small-Medium
- **Impact:** High
- **Confidence:** H — directly fulfills stated site requirement (mailing list #2)
- **Risks:** Fundraising economics need modeling before committing specific margins in the PDF. Use "up to 50% margin — details available upon request."
- **KPI:** 10 nonprofit/org signups before product ships

#### 3. 5-Second Hero — "The EF System"
- **Description:** Hero section leads with a horizontal EF-scale bar (amber EF-1 to near-black EF-5), rendered jar at each level. Single line: "Finally, a heat scale that means something." Sub-copy: "EF-1 (mild) to EF-5 (extreme) — every jar Scoville-rated, every batch consistent."
- **User need:** Heat inconsistency is the #1 consumer pain point (HIGH severity). Leading with the solution differentiates from every competitor in under 5 seconds.
- **Evidence:** "No FDA/USDA standard for mild/medium/hot — consumers frustrated by inconsistency" (Research, Pain Points #1)
- **Effort:** Medium (requires rendered product mockups — shared dependency)
- **Impact:** High
- **Confidence:** H — strongest differentiator, highest-visibility placement

---

#### 4. Mission Page — "The Storm That Started It All"
- **Description:** Origin story told in full (Max, the tornado warning, the mispronunciation). Below: three panels — (1) The EF system and why heat consistency matters, (2) Giving structure with specific percentage, named partner, dollar amounts, (3) Map of impacted communities. Tone shifts from warm/playful above to earnest/specific below.
- **User need:** Conscious Buyer (Jennifer) "will scrutinize the giving structure — if she trusts the brand, becomes a repeat buyer AND advocate. If vague, won't buy again."
- **Evidence:** "Cause marketing skepticism rising — authenticity must be structural, not performative" (Research, Headwinds #4); 41% purchase due to cause association.
- **Effort:** Medium (primarily copywriting; charity partner must be confirmed)
- **Impact:** High
- **Confidence:** H — but BLOCKED until charity partner is named
- **Risks:** Do not launch this page with vague partner language. Name a specific organization.

#### 5. "Coming Soon" Product Pages with Flavor Teasers
- **Description:** Each EF level shows: rendered jar mockup, flavor headline, Scoville range, two key ingredients, and "Notify Me When Available" email capture. Full flavor copy written — feels like a preview, not a placeholder.
- **User need:** Creates urgency and desire pre-launch. Captures the exact consumers most likely to buy on day one.
- **Evidence:** "83% of millennials and 77% of Gen Z make specialty food purchases" driven by craft/story.
- **Effort:** Medium (requires mockup renders + email capture integration)
- **Impact:** High
- **Confidence:** H
- **KPI:** 500+ consumer "notify me" signups in Gulf Coast FL zip codes

#### 11. Dual Mailing List — Context-Sensitive Signup
- **Description:** Context-specific signup prompts throughout the site. Product pages: "Be first to know when we launch." Mission page: "Follow our giving impact." Retailer page: "Get wholesale pricing." Nonprofit page: "Get fundraising program details." Footer has combined signup with segmentation dropdown. Three-list architecture: consumer, retailer, nonprofit.
- **User need:** Retailers and nonprofits have completely different motivations from consumers. A single newsletter undermines professionalism with both audiences.
- **Evidence:** Two mailing lists required (Context); research documents distinct audience motivations.
- **Effort:** Small (email platform setup with list segmentation)
- **Impact:** High (list quality drives launch success)
- **Confidence:** H

#### 12. Primary Tagline: "Chase the storm."
- **Description:** Three words. Active, aspirational, maps to the EF escalation journey. Works as both tagline and CTA on jars/ads/merch/press. Secondary taglines for specific contexts: "Brace yourself." (social), "From the storm that named us." (mission page), "Born in a storm. Built for your table." (origin story).
- **User need:** The brand needs a proprietary, ownable tagline that communicates its primary differentiator.
- **Evidence:** EF-scale system is the brand's uncontested positioning; no competitor uses weather-system rating.
- **Effort:** Small (decision, not development)
- **Impact:** High (governs all copywriting)
- **Confidence:** H

#### 13. Brand Voice: Tone Spectrum + 5 Messaging Pillars
- **Description:** Governing principle: "The product is playful; the mission is earnest. Never mix them up." Five pillars: (1) Heat you can trust (2) Flavor first, always (3) A story worth telling (4) Buy a jar, fund recovery (5) Made for this region.
- **User need:** Two distinct consumer motivations (flavor exploration vs. mission alignment) require two distinct tones on the same site.
- **Evidence:** Flavor Chaser persona (playful) and Conscious Buyer persona (earnest) are validated in research with distinct motivations.
- **Effort:** Small (documentation/decision)
- **Impact:** High (governs all content creation)
- **Confidence:** H

#### 14. Visual Identity System
- **Description:** Three interconnected elements:
  - **Color palette:** Storm charcoal base (#2C2C2C), premium white (#F5F0EB), radar teal accent (#00B4D8). EF gradient: EF-1 warm amber (#D4A017) → EF-2 deep orange (#CC5500) → EF-3 rust red (#8B2500) → EF-4 dark crimson (#5C0A0A) → EF-5 near-black (#1C0A0A).
  - **Typography:** Bold condensed sans or slab for display (weather-alert authority, not farm-stand artisan). Clean grotesque for body. No script fonts.
  - **Storm imagery:** Doppler radar texture, isobar/pressure lines as design elements, stylized vortex logo mark (must work at 16px favicon to embroidery scale).
  - **Five deliberate departures from generic southwestern aesthetics:** No cactus, no desert palette, no handwritten fonts, no pepper illustration as primary element, no state flag imagery.
- **User need:** "Category visually crowded with generic southwestern imagery" (Research, Pain Points #4). Premium salsa labels trending toward bold typography and minimalist design.
- **Evidence:** 2024-2025 label trends; competitive analysis shows all competitors use southwestern or generic food imagery.
- **Effort:** Medium (requires a skilled brand designer)
- **Impact:** High (governs label design, website design, all visual assets)
- **Confidence:** H
- **Risk:** The near-black EF-5 color must read as sophisticated, not threatening. The full gradient should feel like a premium color arc, not a danger warning.

#### 15. "Storm Watch" — Press/Media Page
- **Description:** Minimal press page: brand overview paragraph, high-res product renders (licensed for editorial use), 200-word founder story, brand fact sheet, press inquiry form.

- **Evidence:** Research recommendation: "Launch EF-5 as the PR/media hero — extreme heat + extreme charity = shareable story."
- **Effort:** Small
- **Impact:** Medium-High (earned media multiplier)
- **Confidence:** H

## Strategic Themes

Five cross-cutting themes emerged from ideation:

1. **The EF system IS the brand.** Every idea revolves around it — product names, visual identity, website hero, heat consistency promise, gift sets, social content. If the EF system doesn't land, nothing else matters. Prioritize its communication above all else.

2. **Three lists = one job.** The branding site exists to build three lists: retailers (target: 50), nonprofits (target: 10 campaigns), consumers (target: 500+ in Gulf Coast FL). Every page, every CTA, every design decision should be evaluated against: "Does this help build the lists?"

3. **Playful product, earnest mission.** The tone balance is the brand's secret weapon. Getting it wrong in either direction (too cute about disasters, too serious about salsa) collapses the whole positioning.

4. **Storm imagery, not southwestern imagery.** The visual identity deliberately breaks from the salsa category. Doppler radar, isobar lines, vortex marks — this looks like craft spirits or premium hot sauce, not Pace or Tostitos. Maintaining this distinction requires design discipline.

5. **Fundraising channel is a moat.** No premium salsa brand has built a structured fundraising program. If Tomato Warning establishes this channel early, switching costs for partner organizations are high (custom labels, established relationships, proven campaign results).

## Open Questions

1. **Which charity partner?** Candidates from research: Center for Disaster Philanthropy, Direct Relief, Team Rubicon. Decision needed before mission page.
2. **What specific giving percentage?** Research recommends 5-10% of net profits. Need to model economics.
3. **Trademark availability** for "Tomato Warning," product names (Coastal Calm, Gulf Breeze, Squall Line, Supercell, Ground Zero), and tagline ("Chase the storm.").
4. **Scoville range commitments** — published ranges create accountability. Must be validated during recipe development. Are these ranges achievable and consistent in production?
5. **Social media handles** — @tomatowarning availability across Instagram, Facebook, TikTok, X.
6. **Tech stack for website** — Static site generator, headless CMS, Shopify (for future e-commerce), or something else? Decision needed before build phase.
