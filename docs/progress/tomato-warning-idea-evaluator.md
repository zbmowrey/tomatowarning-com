# Tomato Warning: Idea Evaluation & Ranking

All evaluations reference `docs/research/tomato-warning-research.md` as the evidence base and `docs/progress/tomato-warning-idea-generator.md` as the idea source. Evaluation criteria applied to every idea: market fit, feasibility, strategic alignment, competitive advantage, priority score, risks, and recommendation.

**Phase definitions used throughout:**
- **PURSUE NOW** = Required for the branding site (hero, product line coming soon, retailer CTA, mission, contact, two mailing lists)
- **PURSUE AT LAUNCH** = Should be built when physical product exists; critical for launch success
- **PARK FOR LATER** = Growth-phase or post-launch; valuable but premature
- **REJECT** = Lacks evidence, not viable, or redundant

### PURSUE NOW #1 — Feature 5: Retailer Interest CTA — "Stock the Storm"

**Market fit: H**
The research identifies Publix Local Products program and specialty retailers as the primary distribution path. Building a retailer pipeline before launch is essential for Year 1-2 goals. Evidence: "Publix requires product liability insurance, demonstrated marketing budget, and 'extraordinary product innovation'" (Research, Regional Dynamics). The retailer mailing list is a core pre-launch asset.

**Feasibility: H**
This is a form, a page, and a PDF. Minimal development work. The PDF content (product specs, price points, shelf placement) can be drafted now even without final production details — it signals professionalism and captures buyer interest.

**Strategic alignment: H**
Directly addresses the context requirement: "two mailing lists" for the branding site. The retailer list is one of the two. This is not a nice-to-have; it is a stated site requirement.

**Competitive advantage: H**
No competing salsa startup has a dedicated, professional retailer-facing landing page at the branding site phase. Arriving at a Publix buyer meeting having already captured 50 retailer email signups from the site is a material competitive advantage.

**Priority score: 10/10**
Lowest effort of any high-impact idea. Directly fulfills a stated site requirement. Existential for the distribution strategy.

**Risks:** PDF content requires confirmed product details (pricing, shelf life, case dimensions) that may not be final. Mitigation: use placeholder ranges ("expected MSRP: $7.99–$9.99") with a clear "subject to change" note.

### PURSUE NOW #7 — Tagline: "Rated by the storm."

**Market fit: H**
Directly communicates the EF rating system — the brand's primary differentiator — in four words. Addresses the heat inconsistency pain point implicitly (a rating implies standards). Works across all formats: jar, website, ad, merchandise, press pitch.

**Feasibility: H**
A tagline requires no development work. It requires a decision and brand commitment. The other tagline candidates are evaluated below for completeness.

**Strategic alignment: H**
A tagline is foundational to the branding site. Every page, every headline, every CTA is written in the context of the brand voice. The tagline defines the voice.

**Competitive advantage: H**
No competitor uses a weather-system rating in their brand identity. "Rated by the storm" is proprietary to Tomato Warning — it cannot be credibly used by any other brand. This is a protectable phrase tied to the EF system.

**Priority score: 8/10**
Tagline selection is a prerequisite for everything else. The site cannot be designed in final form without a confirmed tagline.

**Risks/Alternatives evaluated:**
- "Brace yourself." — Clever but generic. Not ownable; many brands use this construction. SECONDARY candidate.
- "Heat you can trust. Flavor you won't forget." — Functional and clear but lacks the brand's personality. Too long for a jar. WEAK.
- "From the storm that named us." — Good for the mission page; too narrow as a brand-wide tagline. SPECIFIC USE ONLY.
- "EF-scale heat. Real-world flavor." — Best for retail signage where clarity beats cleverness. SECONDARY use.
- "Chasing flavor since the forecast." — Social-native; too cute for the primary tagline. SOCIAL USE.
- "Five levels. No apologies." — Too aggressive for brand-wide. Works for EF-4/EF-5 specifically. PRODUCT-LEVEL USE.
- "Born in a storm. Built for your table." — Warm and origin-forward. Best for mission page hero. SPECIFIC USE.
- "The weather forecast you actually want to see." — Too self-amused for a primary tagline. REJECT for primary.
- "Premium salsa with purpose. Just look at the forecast." — Best for email subject lines and pitches. SPECIFIC USE.

**Recommendation on tagline candidates:** "Rated by the storm." as primary. "Brace yourself." as secondary/social-facing. Specific candidates (From the storm that named us / Born in a storm built for your table) reserved for mission page. Others deployed contextually.

### PURSUE NOW #8 — Brand Voice: Tone Spectrum & Earnest/Playful Balance

**Market fit: H**
The research identifies two distinct consumer motivations: flavor exploration (Flavor Chaser, playful) and mission alignment (Conscious Buyer, earnest). A brand voice that collapses these into a single tone will alienate at least one segment. The tone spectrum concept — product pages playful, mission page earnest — directly maps to the segmented consumer research.

**Feasibility: H**
This is a style guide decision, not a development task. It costs nothing to document and governs all copywriting going forward. The failure mode (mixing tones incorrectly) is more costly than getting it right from the start.

**Strategic alignment: H**
Brand voice governs every word on the branding site. This must be established before the site is built, not after.

**Competitive advantage: M**
Most salsa brands have undifferentiated voice — generic "authentic" and "bold" language. The specific structural principle (the product is playful; the mission is earnest; never mix them up) is harder to execute than it sounds and gives the brand a distinctive voice that is internally consistent.

**Priority score: 8/10**
Foundational. Must precede copywriting.

**Risks:** The "never mix them up" principle requires editorial discipline. On a small team, without a defined style guide, tone drift will occur. Mitigation: document the governing principle and two example passages (product voice, mission voice) in a brand guide before any copywriting begins.

### PURSUE NOW #10 — Visual Identity: Color Palette Direction

**Market fit: H**
The research identifies boring/generic branding as a MEDIUM-severity pain point: "Category visually crowded with generic southwestern imagery." The proposed color system (charcoal base, EF gradient from amber to near-black, electric teal accent) is deliberately non-southwestern and directly addresses this gap. The gradient-as-identity concept (consumers identifying EF level by color alone within 2-3 purchases) is a scalable, memory-forming system.

**Feasibility: H**
Color palette selection is a design decision. All five EF colors, base charcoal, premium white, and teal accent are documented with hex values. Execution requires a designer, not a developer.

**Strategic alignment: H**
The color system governs label design, website design, gift box design, and all marketing materials. Establishing it now — before the branding site is designed — prevents costly redesigns later.

**Competitive advantage: H**
Explicitly avoids the five visual clichés of competitors (no cactus, no desert palette, no handwritten fonts, no pepper illustration as primary element, no state flag imagery). This system would look at home next to craft spirits or premium olive oil brands — not next to Pace or Tostitos. That is exactly the positioning goal.

**Priority score: 8/10**
Design foundation. Required before any visual asset is produced.

**Risks:** The near-black EF-5 (#1C0A0A / #2B0000) risks looking visually "scary" in a way that might deter gift buyers from the Full Forecast set. Ensure the full EF gradient is presented as a spectrum (beautiful) not a warning system (threatening). The amber-to-black arc should read as sophisticated color design, not danger signage.

**Market fit: H**
Premium trends in the research explicitly note: "Bold, expressive typography as primary visual element; minimalist/clean design with whitespace — signals sophistication." **Competitive advantage: H**
The prohibition on script/handwritten fonts deliberately departs from the "farm stand artisan" aesthetic that dominates the premium salsa category. Bold condensed display type more closely references craft spirits, craft brewing, and premium hot sauce brands — the reference category that Tomato Warning is choosing to compete in culturally.

**Priority score: 7/10**
Required before label design. Zero development cost. Design decision only.

**Risks:** "Weather alert" typography can skew toward generic/corporate if not executed carefully. The Dharma Gothic reference is strong; the execution should feel editorial (magazine cover) not utilitarian (government poster). Requires a skilled designer who understands the positioning.

## PURSUE NOW — Core EF-Scale Product Line

The following five product ideas are PURSUE NOW because they define the "product line (coming soon)" section required for the branding site. Physical product doesn't exist yet; the names, flavor profiles, and heat commitments appear on the site as pre-launch content.

### Product: EF-1 Coastal Calm

**Market fit: H**
Gateway SKU for the health-conscious ingredient reader and casual buyers. Addresses heat inconsistency pain point — EF-1 with a committed 500–1,500 SHU range answers the documented consumer frustration about "mild" being unpredictable across brands.

**Feasibility: H** (for branding site phase)
Naming and flavor profile are fully specified. The name "Coastal Calm" is regionally evocative without being generic. "A gentle gust — big flavor, little fire" is an effective label tagline.

**Strategic alignment: H**
Required for the product line page. Without EF-1, the scale is incomplete and the entry-level buyer has no on-ramp.

**Competitive advantage: H**
The committed Scoville range (500–1,500 SHU) published on the label is not done by any competitor. That commitment alone differentiates this "mild" salsa from every generic mild on the market.

**Priority score: 9/10**

**Risks:** The poblano + fresh herb combination may be perceived as a departure from traditional salsa flavor profiles. This is a feature (differentiation), but the flavor must genuinely deliver on the "big flavor" promise — otherwise the brand's credibility for the entire EF system is undermined from the mildest SKU.

### Product: EF-2 Gulf Breeze

**Market fit: H**
Likely the highest-volume SKU at retail — the "medium" sweet spot where most salsa buyers live. The chipotle smoke depth directly addresses the flavor-dumbing-down pain point that separates Tomato Warning from generic "medium" salsas.

**Feasibility: H** (for branding site phase)
Name and profile are fully specified. Chipotle in adobo is a standard ingredient with reliable sourcing. The 1,500–5,000 SHU range is widely understood.

**Strategic alignment: H**
Required for the product line page. The commercial center of gravity of the lineup.

**Competitive advantage: H**
Most competing "medium" salsas live in this heat range but lack the chipotle smoke character. The flavor identity (smoke + heat + brightness) is defensible even if the range itself is mainstream.

**Priority score: 9/10**

**Risks:** Chipotle in adobo is a commonly available flavor profile. The flavor narrative on the label and product page must be strong enough to justify premium pricing over mass-market alternatives with similar smoke notes. This is primarily a marketing execution risk.

---

### Product: EF-3 Squall Line

**Market fit: H**
The research explicitly names EF-3 as the Marcus persona's first purchase. The habanero-tropical combination (mango or pineapple) sits in the "gateway heat" category that has enormous social media and gifting appeal. The EF-3 is also the gift-set anchor for the Storm Sampler bundle.

**Feasibility: H** (for branding site phase)
Name and flavor profile fully specified. Habanero-fruit combinations are well-understood production territory. The 5,000–25,000 SHU range is achievable and distinctive.

**Strategic alignment: H**
Required for the product line page. This is the brand's most socially shareable flavor combination.

**Competitive advantage: H**
Habanero-mango is not uncommon in hot sauce but is unusual in salsa. The EF-3 "gateway heat" positioning is explicitly validated by the Marcus persona in the research.

**Priority score: 9/10**

**Risks:** Fruit ingredient (mango/pineapple) creates co-packer complexity and seasonal sourcing challenges. This is a production risk, not a branding site risk. For the "coming soon" product page, the concept is sound. Resolve the mango vs. pineapple question before committing to final recipe — both work, but picking one sharpens the flavor story.

---

### Product: EF-4 Supercell

**Market fit: H**
Ghost pepper was Mrs. Renfro's fastest-growing product ever. EF-4 is the premium version of that proven commercial concept with black garlic for umami complexity. This SKU drives trial of the full lineup — serious heat buyers will start here and work backward.

**Feasibility: H** (for branding site phase)
Ghost pepper sourcing is established commercial territory. The concept is fully specified. The 25,000–100,000 SHU range is clearly extreme enough to be taken seriously without the liability concerns of EF-5.

**Strategic alignment: H**
Required for the product line page. This is the heat-authority SKU — it validates the brand's credibility with heat adventurers.

**Competitive advantage: H**
The black garlic umami angle differentiates from ghost pepper novelty plays like Mrs. Renfro's. This is ghost pepper elevated — not just hot, but complex.

**Priority score: 9/10**

**Risks:** Ghost pepper handling in production requires safety protocols (PPE, ventilation). This is an operational consideration for production, not a branding risk. Black garlic is a premium ingredient that must be reflected in the price positioning.

---

### Product: EF-5 Ground Zero

**Market fit: H**
The research explicitly recommends EF-5 as the "PR/media hero — extreme heat + extreme charity = shareable story." Carolina Reaper and Trinidad Moruga Scorpion are established premium heat credentials. The "designed to have actual salsa character, not just shock value" positioning is exactly the right differentiation from gimmick heat products.

**Feasibility: H** (for branding site phase)
The concept and safety labeling are fully specified. The "Not responsible for consequences" label line is both legally protective and brand-appropriate.

**Strategic alignment: H**
Required for the product line page. EF-5 is the aspirational cap of the system — without it, EF-4 feels like the ceiling and the brand's "extreme" positioning is undersold.

**Competitive advantage: H**
The research recommendation is unambiguous: "Consider launching EF-5 as the PR/media hero." No competing brand uses a structured Reaper/Scorpion product as part of a systematic heat scale.

**Priority score: 9/10**

**Risks:** This is the highest-risk SKU from a production, liability, and consumer safety perspective. The media attention it generates will also attract heat challengers who may have negative physical reactions. Liability labeling ("serve in small amounts, dairy nearby recommended") is appropriate but should be reviewed by an attorney before launch. This risk is a production-phase concern — for the branding site "coming soon" presentation, the concept is sound and the safety note is already drafted.
