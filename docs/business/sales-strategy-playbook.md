# Tomato Warning: Sales Strategy & Playbook

## Sales Philosophy

### How We Sell

We sell the way a storm chaser tells you about the one that changed everything: with conviction, respect, and a story you can't unhear.

Tomato Warning is not a commodity. It is a premium, story-driven product that solves a real problem (inconsistent heat labeling), delivers an exceptional experience (flavor-first formulation), and funds a meaningful mission (disaster relief). Our sales approach reflects all three of these truths.

**Three principles govern every sale:**

1. **Lead with flavor, follow with mission.** The salsa must earn the purchase on taste alone. The mission earns the loyalty. Never lead with guilt. Never lead with charity. Lead with "try this" and let the product do its job.

2. **Consultative, never manipulative.** We ask questions. We listen. We recommend the right EF level for the right person. We would rather sell someone one jar of EF-1 Coastal Calm that they love than push them into EF-4 Supercell for a story they'll regret. A satisfied customer comes back. A burned customer doesn't.

3. **Every interaction ends with a clear next step.** Whether it's "try a sample," "take my card," "visit tomatowarning.com," or "I'll send you the fundraiser kit" — no conversation ends without a path forward. Not pressure. A path.

### Who We Are Selling To

We serve three channels, each with distinct buyers and motivations:

| Channel | Primary Buyer | What They Want | How We Win |
|---------|--------------|----------------|------------|
| **DTC (website)** | Flavor-Forward Foodies, Gift Buyers | Discovery, convenience, the full story | Brand experience, variety packs, email nurture |
| **Retail** | Store buyers (Publix, specialty) | Margin, velocity, differentiation | Sell sheet, demo data, unique shelf presence |
| **Fundraiser** | Organizers (Coach Dave persona) | High margin, easy logistics, product that sells itself | Turnkey kit, no upfront cost, shelf-stable premium product |

## Channel Strategy: Retail

### The Retail Ladder

Retail is a ladder, not a leap. Each rung builds the credibility and data for the next.

**Rung 1: Farmers Markets & Local Events (Months 1-6)**
- 2-4 weekly farmers markets in Gulf Coast FL (Sarasota, Tampa, Fort Myers, Naples)
- Purpose: Direct customer feedback, demand data, brand awareness, sales volume
- Target: $500-$1,500/weekend across all markets
- Capture: Email signups, social media follows, product reviews

**Rung 2: Specialty Stores & Gourmet Shops (Months 3-9)**
- 5-15 independent specialty stores, gift shops, gourmet markets
- Detwiler's Farm Market, local co-ops, beach town gift shops
- Purpose: Establish retail velocity data, test shelf placement, build store-level relationships
- Terms: Net 30, consignment for first order if needed to get on shelf

**Rung 3: Regional Grocery — Publix Local Products (Months 9-18)**
- Apply to Publix Local Products program with traction data from Rungs 1-2
- Demonstrate: consistent sales velocity, marketing budget, product liability insurance, "extraordinary product innovation" (the EF-scale system IS the innovation)
- Target: 10-25 Publix stores in Gulf Coast FL region initially
- Purpose: Validate mainstream grocery viability, build case for expansion

**Rung 4: Multi-State Regional (Year 2-3)**
- H-E-B (TX), Whole Foods (regional), Fresh Market, Sprouts
- Leverage Publix success data + DTC traction

### The Retail Buyer Pitch

Every retail buyer meeting follows this framework:

**The 3-Minute Pitch:**

> "I'm going to tell you three things about Tomato Warning, and then I want to hear what matters most to you.
>
> **First, the product.** Five premium salsas rated on the Enhanced Fujita tornado scale — EF-1 through EF-5, from 500 Scoville to over 500,000. Every jar has a published heat range, so your customers know exactly what they're getting. No more 'medium' that's too hot or 'hot' that's too mild. We solved that problem.
>
> **Second, the story.** The name came from a three-year-old kid during a Florida storm who said 'Tomato Warning' instead of 'Tornado Warning.' It stuck. Now we make salsas rated like storms and we donate at least 10% of net profits to disaster relief organizations. It's not a marketing gimmick — it's why we exist.
>
> **Third, the shelf.** Look at your salsa aisle right now. What do you see? Southwestern imagery. Cowboy hats. Generic 'artisan' labels. Now picture five jars with storm charcoal labels, gold foil, and a graduated color system from amber to near-black. Your customers will stop. They'll pick it up. And when they read the back, they'll buy it.
>
> What matters most to you — the product differentiation, the brand story, or the shelf impact?"

**After the pitch, listen.** The buyer will tell you what they care about. Match your follow-up to their priority.

## Channel Strategy: Fundraiser Partnerships

### Why This Channel Wins

The fundraiser channel is the sleeper hit of this business. Here's why:

1. **Product-market fit is exceptional.** Shelf-stable (no melting chocolate, no frozen cookie dough). Premium (people feel good buying it). Mission-aligned (disaster relief resonates with community-minded buyers).

2. **Coach Dave is our ideal partner.** Youth sports coaches, PTA presidents, scout leaders — they organize 1-2 fundraisers per season, need $800-$1,200 raised, and are desperate for products that don't require freezers or guilt.

3. **Dual-purpose pitch.** "Buy salsa your family will actually eat, AND the money goes to your kid's soccer team AND disaster relief." Three wins in one purchase. That's a close that writes itself.

*Note: Fundraiser pricing is set slightly above DTC to account for partner margin while keeping buyer price reasonable. The premium positioning justifies the price — this isn't $5 candy bars.*

### Fundraiser Partner Onboarding Flow

**Step 1: First Contact (Day 1)**
Partner reaches out via website form, referral, or outbound prospecting.

**Step 2: Discovery Call (Day 2-3)**
15-minute call to understand:
- Organization type (sports team, school, nonprofit, scouts)
- Fundraising goal ($)
- Timeline (campaign dates)
- Group size (number of sellers)
- Past fundraising experience (what worked, what didn't)

**Step 3: Kit Delivery (Day 5-7)**

**Step 4: Campaign Launch (Day 7-21)**
- Typical campaign window: 2-3 weeks
- Partners distribute order forms and share digital links
- Tomato Warning provides a custom online ordering page per partner (orders ship direct or bulk to partner)

**Step 5: Fulfillment & Payment (Day 21-28)**
- Orders consolidated and shipped
- Partner receives their 50% within 14 days of campaign close
- Follow-up survey to partner for feedback

**Step 6: Retention (Ongoing)**
- Thank-you note + impact update ("Your campaign helped fund $X in disaster relief")
- Re-engagement for next season: "Ready to run it again?"
- Referral program: partner refers another org, both get $25 product credit

## Objection Handling Master List

These are the top objections across all channels and the recommended responses. Memorize the framework, personalize the delivery.

### Price Objections

**1. "It's too expensive for salsa."**
> "I hear you — and you're right that you can get a jar of Pace for $3. But you're not comparing the same thing. This is small-batch, premium ingredients, published Scoville ranges so you know exactly what you're getting, and 10% of net profits go to disaster relief. Think of it less like grocery salsa and more like a craft beer or artisan cheese. You'd pay $8-10 for either of those without thinking twice."

**2. "I can get salsa cheaper at Costco."**
> "Absolutely. And if you're buying salsa by the gallon for a Super Bowl party, that's a great choice. But if you want something you're proud to put on the table — something with real flavor, a real story, and a real mission — that's a different purchase. Most of our customers have both in their fridge. We're the one they reach for when it matters."

### Product / Quality Objections

**3. "I don't like hot sauce / I can't handle heat."**
> "That's exactly who EF-1 Coastal Calm is for. It's 500 Scoville — barely any heat at all, just incredible flavor. Poblano, cilantro, lime, Florida tomatoes. Think of it as a premium salsa that happens to have a hint of warmth. Try a sample — I think you'll be surprised."

**4. "How is this different from every other artisan salsa?"**
> "Two things no one else does. First, the EF-scale: every jar has a published heat range in Scoville units, so you know exactly what you're getting — no more guessing if 'medium' means the same thing between brands. Second, at least 10% of net profits go directly to named disaster relief organizations. No other salsa brand does both."

### Trust / Brand Objections

**5. "I've never heard of this brand."**
> "You're hearing about it right now — and that's how every brand starts. The difference is you're hearing about it directly from the people who make it. Try a sample. If the salsa speaks for itself, that's all the reputation we need."

**6. "How do I know the charity part is real?"**
> "Great question — and you should ask it. We name our partner organizations on our website. We publish annual giving reports with actual dollar amounts. And the whole brand started because a kid said 'Tomato Warning' during a real Florida storm — the mission is why we exist, not something we bolted on for marketing. Scan the QR code on the jar and see for yourself."

### Competition Objections

**7. "I already have a hot sauce I love."**
> "Keep it. This isn't a replacement — it's a salsa, not a hot sauce. Different occasion entirely. Your hot sauce goes on wings and eggs. This goes on chips, tacos, grilled fish, and anything that deserves real flavor depth. Most of our customers use both."

### Fundraiser-Specific Objections

**8. "Fundraiser salsa? That's weird."**
> "That's what makes it work. Everyone's seen the candy bars, the cookie dough, the wrapping paper. This is different — it's premium, it's shelf-stable, and when someone asks 'What's this?', your sellers get to tell a story about storms and community instead of just asking for $5. Different is what gets people to open their wallets."

**9. "Can my team really sell $10 salsa?"**
> "Here's the thing: they're not selling salsa. They're selling a story about a kid who said 'Tomato Warning' during a storm, a family that turned it into a brand, and a mission to help communities recover from severe weather. The salsa just happens to be incredible. When the story is that good, the price takes care of itself."

**10. "$10/jar means my families have to spend a lot."**
> "Actually, most families spend $20-30 total, which is in line with what they'd spend on cookie dough or wrapping paper. The difference is they get a premium product they'll actually use and enjoy, not something that sits in the pantry. And the 3-pack at $27 is a natural upsell — three heat levels for the price of a decent lunch."

## Pricing Defense

### Why $7.99-$9.99 Is the Right Price

The price is not aspirational. It is justified by five concrete factors:

1. **Premium ingredients.** Florida tomatoes, habaneros, ghost peppers, Carolina Reapers, black garlic, tropical mango. These are not commodity inputs.

2. **Glass packaging.** 16 oz glass jars, not plastic tubs. Glass preserves flavor, signals quality, and is reusable/recyclable.

3. **Scoville commitment.** Every jar has a published heat range. This requires consistent sourcing, testing, and quality control that mass brands skip.

4. **Charitable mission.** At least 10% of net profits to disaster relief. The customer is buying flavor AND funding community recovery.

5. **Competitive positioning.** Stonewall Kitchen: $7-$15. Frontera: $6-$8. Mrs. Renfro's: $5-$7. Mateo's: $7-$10. We are squarely in the premium tier with stronger differentiation than any of them. No other salsa brand combines a systematic heat rating, published Scoville ranges, a verifiable charitable mission, and an origin story that directly connects all three. *(See Messaging Framework, Section 7 for full competitive positioning statements.)*

### The Price Conversation Framework

When price comes up, use this three-step framework:

1. **Acknowledge.** "I get it. Eight dollars is more than the jar you're used to."
2. **Reframe.** "But think about what's in this jar — [specific ingredients for their EF level], a heat rating you can trust, and a mission that actually helps people."
3. **Compare.** "You'd pay $8 for a craft beer you'd finish in 10 minutes. This jar lasts weeks and makes every meal better. That's pretty good math."

That kid's mispronunciation is helping communities recover from real storms."

### The Mission Pitch (15-Second Version)

> "Tomato Warning donates at least 10% of net profits to named disaster relief organizations — the teams that put boots on the ground when storms hit. Great salsa, real impact."

### Pre-Profit Mission Language (Year 1)

In Year 1, net profits may be zero or negative. The charitable commitment is to 10% of net profits, so there may not be a donation to report yet. When customers ask about the mission early on:

**Approved language:**
> "Our commitment is at least 10% of net profits to disaster relief, reported annually. As a new brand, we're building toward that first report. In the meantime, our fundraiser program already provides salsa at cost to local schools and teams — that's community investment happening right now."

**Never say:**
- "Every jar helps disaster relief" (not true until profitable)
- "A portion of your purchase goes to charity" (misleading pre-profit)
- Any per-jar donation calculation unless mathematically verified
