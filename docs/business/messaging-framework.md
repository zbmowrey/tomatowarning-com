# Tomato Warning: Messaging Framework & Copy Guidelines

## Document Purpose

This is the operational messaging playbook for Tomato Warning. Every piece of communication — website copy, email, social post, retail pitch, fundraiser flyer — should be traceable to the frameworks in this document. Writers should be able to open this and immediately produce on-brand copy without guessing.

---

## 1. Core Brand Messages

Every Tomato Warning communication should reinforce at least one of these five messages. No piece of content should contradict any of them.

### Message 1: Heat You Can Trust
**Statement:** Tomato Warning rates every salsa on the Enhanced Fujita scale (EF-1 to EF-5) with published Scoville ranges, so you always know exactly what you're getting.
**Why it matters:** No FDA/USDA standard exists for "mild," "medium," or "hot." Consumers are frustrated by inconsistency across brands. Our EF scale with published Scoville ranges solves a real problem.
**Proof points:** Published SHU ranges per level (500 to 500,000+), five distinct heat tiers, consistent batch testing.

### Message 2: Flavor First, Always
**Statement:** Every EF level is built around a distinct, complex flavor profile — not just a heat number. We craft flavor, then calibrate heat.
**Why it matters:** Most hot sauces and premium salsas at extreme heat levels sacrifice flavor for intensity. Our product line proves you don't have to choose.
**Proof points:** Named, specific ingredients at every level (black garlic in EF-4, tropical mango in EF-3, chipotle smoke in EF-2). Food pairings designed for each level.

### Message 3: A Story Worth Telling
**Statement:** Tomato Warning was born when a three-year-old boy heard "tornado warning" during a Florida storm and said, "Daddy, there's a Tomato Warning."
**Why it matters:** The origin story is authentic, emotionally resonant, and impossible to manufacture. It connects directly to our storm theming and charitable mission. It makes people smile and then it makes them care.
**Proof points:** The origin story itself. The EF naming system. The storm-themed product names and narratives.

### Message 4: Buy a Jar, Fund Recovery
**Statement:** At least 10% of net profits go directly to regional disaster relief organizations — named partners, specific dollars, reported annually.
**Why it matters:** 41% of consumers purchase because of cause association. But skepticism is rising. Our commitment is specific (10% of net profits), verifiable (named partners), and reported (annual public disclosure). This isn't cause marketing — it's structural commitment.
**Proof points:** Named charity partners, annual dollar reporting, origin story directly connected to disaster experience.

### Message 5: Made for This Region
**Statement:** Tomato Warning is Florida-made, Gulf Coast-born, and built for the communities that know storms best.
**Why it matters:** Regional identity is a commercial asset. Publix's Local Products program, H-E-B's local brand support, and consumer preference for local producers all validate this. And it's authentic — the brand literally came from a storm.
**Proof points:** Florida production, Gulf Coast launch, storm-culture connection, tornado alley expansion plan.

## 8. Psychological Principles

Each messaging approach is grounded in behavioral science. Writers should understand WHY these work, not just WHAT to write.

### Identity
**Principle:** People buy products that reinforce who they believe they are or who they want to become.
**Application:** "What's your EF level?" positions the product as a reflection of personal identity. Marcus (the Flavor Chaser) doesn't buy salsa — he buys confirmation that he's an adventurous eater. The EF scale gives him a label to claim.
**Where to use:** Social media, product quizzes, community building, UGC prompts.
**Example:** "EF-4 people know: heat should taste like something."

### Social Proof
**Principle:** People follow the behavior of others, especially peers they identify with.
**Application:** Customer reviews, UGC (user-generated content), "X jars sold," community hashtags, influencer partnerships. The EF-5 challenge content is inherently social — people film themselves trying it.
**Where to use:** Product pages (reviews), social media (UGC reposts), email (community highlights), retail (shelf talkers with ratings).
**Example:** "Join 2,000+ families who've found their EF level."

### Scarcity
**Principle:** People value what's limited or might run out.
**Application:** Small-batch production is genuinely limited. Seasonal releases, limited runs, "back in stock" notifications all create authentic scarcity — don't manufacture it. Fundraiser campaigns have natural time windows.
**Where to use:** Product launches, restock alerts, fundraiser timelines, seasonal campaigns.
**Example:** "Small batch. When it's gone, it's gone." / "This fundraiser ends Friday."

### Authority
**Principle:** People trust credible, specific, verifiable claims over vague assertions.
**Application:** Published Scoville ranges (not "hot" — "25,000-100,000 SHU"). Named charity partners (not "disaster relief" — "[specific organization name]"). Specific ingredient lists. Annual giving reports with dollar amounts. Jennifer (Conscious Buyer) especially responds to this.
**Where to use:** Product labels, mission page, press releases, retail buyer pitches, fundraiser materials.
**Example:** "10% of net profits. Named partners. Reported annually."

### Reciprocity
**Principle:** When someone gives you something, you feel compelled to give back.
**Application:** The charitable mission creates a reciprocity loop — "this brand does good, so I'll support them." Free samples at farmers markets create trial-to-purchase reciprocity. Fundraiser pricing (at-cost salsa for organizations) creates goodwill reciprocity.
**Where to use:** Mission messaging, sampling programs, fundraiser partnerships, community events.
**Example:** "Every jar helps communities recover. So go ahead — enjoy the salsa."

### Commitment & Consistency
**Principle:** Once people take a small action, they're more likely to take bigger ones consistent with that identity.
**Application:** Buy EF-1 first (low commitment), then explore EF-2, then EF-3... The product line is designed as an escalation path. Email signup (small commitment) leads to first purchase leads to subscription leads to gift sets leads to advocacy.
**Where to use:** Product lineup page (start here, go further), email sequences, loyalty programs, variety pack upsells.
**Example:** "You started at EF-1. Ready for what's next?"

## 10. Anti-Messages: What We Never Say

| Never Say | Why | Say Instead |
|-----------|-----|-------------|
| "The best salsa in Florida" | Unverifiable superlative. By what measure? | "Premium Florida-made salsa" or "Gulf Coast's storm-rated salsa" |
| "All-natural" | FDA hasn't formally defined this term. Legal risk. | "Made with real, recognizable ingredients" |
| "Hottest sauce on the market" | We can't test every competitor. EF-5 is extreme but the claim is indefensible. | "Rated EF-5: 100,000-500,000+ SHU. You were warned." |
| "A portion of proceeds" | Vague. Jennifer (Conscious Buyer) will walk. Cause-marketing skepticism is rising. | "10% of net profits to named disaster relief organizations" |
| "We're not like other hot sauces" | Generic differentiation claim that every brand makes. | State the specific difference: "Five heat levels with published Scoville ranges" |
| "Artisanal" / "Craft" without context | Overused to the point of meaninglessness in food marketing. | "Small-batch" + specific detail: "Small-batch, Florida-made, glass-jarred" |
| "Spice up your life" | Cliche. Below the brand's voice standard. | Use storm metaphor: "Bring the storm to your table" |
| "Burns so good" | Pain-focused language contradicts "flavor first, always." | "Heat with depth" / "Relentless but rewarding" |
| "Mild / Medium / Hot" | Our entire brand exists because these terms are meaningless. Using them undermines the EF scale. | Always use EF ratings: "EF-1" through "EF-5" |
| "Charity brand" or "We're a charity" | We're a for-profit brand with a charitable mission. Misrepresenting this is legally and ethically wrong. | "Mission-driven" / "10% of net profits fund disaster relief" |
| "Guaranteed to..." | Legal exposure. Product experience is subjective. | "Designed to..." / "Crafted for..." |
| Any specific donation dollar amount (unless verified and current) | If the number changes or was wrong, trust is destroyed permanently. | "10% of net profits" (the percentage commitment, not the dollar amount, unless publishing verified annual reports) |

## 11. Product Line Architecture: The System Thinking

The five EF levels are not five independent products. They are a system designed to drive trial, exploration, collection, and loyalty.

### The Escalation Path
**EF-1 to EF-5 is a journey.** A customer who starts at EF-1 should feel naturally pulled toward EF-2, then EF-3. Each level reveals something new: smoke at EF-2, tropical heat at EF-3, umami depth at EF-4, devastation at EF-5. The line rewards exploration.

**Messaging implication:** Never position a single product in isolation. Always reference the scale. "Coastal Calm, EF-1" is better than just "Coastal Calm" because it implies there's more to discover.

### The Collection Impulse
**Five levels = five labels = five colors.** The visual gradient from warm amber (EF-1) to near-black (EF-5) is designed to look incomplete when you only own three. Gift sets and variety packs exploit this — "the full storm" is all five.

**Messaging implication:** Variety packs and gift sets should be positioned as "the complete experience," not a discount bundle.

### The Challenge Dynamic
**EF-5 is not for everyone — and that's the point.** Ground Zero exists to be aspirational, talked about, and occasionally survived. It drives social content, word-of-mouth, and brand awareness even for people who never buy it.

**Messaging implication:** EF-5 messaging should be dramatic and exclusive. "Total devastation. You were warned." It's the hero product for PR, media, and social content — even if EF-2 and EF-3 are the volume sellers.

### The Entry Points
Different customers enter at different levels:
- **EF-1:** Heat-averse but flavor-curious. "I don't even like hot sauce."
- **EF-2:** Everyday hot sauce user. "I like some heat."
- **EF-3:** Adventurous eater. "I want to feel something."
- **EF-4:** Heat enthusiast. "I respect heat."
- **EF-5:** Challenge seeker. "I dare you."

**Messaging implication:** Don't assume everyone starts at EF-1. Product page copy and social content should speak to each entry point independently.

## 12. Food Pairing Strategy

Food pairings are not suggestions — they are product positioning. When we say "EF-4 pairs with charred ribeye," we're positioning Supercell as a premium steak companion, not a condiment. This elevates perceived value and purchase occasion.

### Pairing Principles
1. **Match intensity to occasion.** EF-1 pairs with light, fresh foods (fish tacos, eggs). EF-4 pairs with bold, rich foods (ribeye, short ribs). The pairing should feel natural, not forced.
2. **Elevate the meal, don't just add heat.** The pairing should make the food better, not just hotter. "EF-2 adds smoky depth to pulled pork" is better than "put EF-2 on pulled pork."
3. **Include a drink pairing.** A cold lager with EF-2. A spicy margarita with EF-3. A smoky mezcal with EF-4. This positions the product as part of a complete experience.
4. **Include one unexpected pairing per product.** Dark chocolate + aged cheddar with EF-4. Fresh oysters with EF-1. These create conversation and content.

## Appendix: Voice Quick-Reference

When writing any Tomato Warning copy, check against these rules:

1. **The product is playful; the mission is earnest.** Never mix them up. Don't joke about disaster relief. Don't be solemn about salsa.
2. **Use storm language, not generic spice language.** "The storm is building" not "things are heating up." "Rated by the storm" not "rated by heat."
3. **Be specific, never vague.** "25,000-100,000 SHU" not "really hot." "10% of net profits" not "a portion of proceeds." "Ghost pepper + black garlic" not "premium ingredients."
4. **Respect every EF level.** EF-1 is not "the weak one." It's "the pristine morning before the storm." Every level has its audience and its dignity.
5. **Never use the words mild, medium, or hot.** We built the EF scale to replace these meaningless terms. Use them and you undermine the entire brand.
6. **Lead with flavor, follow with heat, close with mission.** This is the priority order for most communications. Taste is the qualifier, heat is the differentiator, mission is the loyalty driver.
