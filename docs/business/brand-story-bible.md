# Tomato Warning: Brand Story Bible

*The definitive narrative architecture for everything Tomato Warning says, writes, films, posts, and believes.*

---

## 1. The Origin Myth

The best brand stories are not invented. They are remembered. Tomato Warning's origin is a true story, and that truth is
our most powerful asset. Below, the same story in four lengths -- each crafted for a different moment, but all carrying
the same emotional core: **innocence becoming purpose.**

### The 15-Second Version

*For social media captions, elevator encounters, and the back of a business card.*

> A three-year-old heard "Tornado Warning" on TV and said "Tomato Warning." His family moved to Florida, lived through
> real
> storms, and turned that mispronunciation into a premium salsa brand that funds disaster relief. Every jar is rated on
> the tornado scale. Every purchase helps communities recover.

### The 60-Second Version

*For farmers market conversations, retail buyer meetings, and podcast intros.*

> During a summer storm in Southern Illinois, a three-year-old boy named Max looked up at his dad and said, "Daddy,
> there's a Tomato Warning."

> He meant tornado. But the name stuck -- first as a family joke, then as something more.

> When the family moved to Florida, storms followed. Hurricanes on the Gulf Coast. Devastation across the Southeast.
> Tornado season in the heartland. Watching communities lose everything and rebuild from nothing changed the meaning of
> that innocent mispronunciation.

> Tomato Warning became a premium salsa brand rated on the Enhanced Fujita tornado scale -- EF-1 through EF-5, from a
> gentle coastal breeze to total devastation. Five salsas. Five storms. And at least 10% of net profits go directly to
> disaster relief organizations helping communities recover from the real thing.

> A kid said the wrong word at the right time. Now it means something.

### The 3-Minute Version

*For the website About page, press features, and video script foundation.*

> It was a summer evening in Southern Illinois. The kind where the sky turns that particular shade of green-gray and the
> air goes still before it goes violent. A tornado warning scrolled across the bottom of the television screen. The
> sirens
> hadn't started yet, but the alert was there, doing what alerts do -- demanding attention.

> Max was three. He was watching the screen with the half-attention of a toddler, absorbing the shapes and sounds the
> way kids do -- filtering the world through a brain still building its dictionary. He looked up at his dad and said,
> with
> total confidence: *"Daddy, there's a Tomato Warning."*

> It was the kind of moment that makes you laugh even when you probably shouldn't -- a child's voice cutting through the
> tension of a storm with something so perfectly, absurdly wrong that it felt right. The family laughed. The storm
> passed.
> The phrase became one of those family stories you tell at dinners and holidays, the kind that gets funnier every time
> because it captures something real about how children see the world: fearlessly, and without any of the baggage adults
> carry.

> Then the family moved to Florida.

> And the storms followed.

> Gulf Coast hurricanes. The kind that don't just knock out power -- they erase neighborhoods. The family watched
> communities in their own backyard lose homes, schools, businesses. They watched volunteers fill gaps that governments
> couldn't fill fast enough. They saw devastation in eastern Tennessee, in western North Carolina -- places where people
> didn't expect to learn the word "catastrophic" from personal experience.

> Somewhere in that accumulation of weather and witness, a mispronunciation became a mission.

> Tomato Warning is a premium salsa brand rated on the Enhanced Fujita tornado scale -- the same scale meteorologists
> use to classify the destructive power of tornadoes. Five products, EF-1 through EF-5, from the bright, breezy calm of
> Coastal Calm to the catastrophic heat of Ground Zero. Each one crafted with intention: real ingredients, honest heat,
> and flavors designed to be remembered.

> But the salsas are only half the story.

> At least 10% of Tomato Warning's net profits go directly to disaster relief -- to the regional organizations putting
> boots on the ground when storms take everything. Not vague "a portion of proceeds." Named partners. Real dollars.
> Public
> accounting.

> Because a three-year-old heard a warning on TV and turned it into something his family couldn't forget. And his family
> decided that if a storm was going to name their brand, the least they could do was help people survive the real ones.

> Tomato Warning. Chase the storm.

### The Full Narrative

*For the internal brand bible, culture document, and founding mythology.*

> Every brand has an origin story. Most of them are manufactured in conference rooms by people who understand that
> consumers buy narratives as much as they buy products. Focus-grouped. Workshopped. Optimized for shareability.

> This is not one of those stories.

> Tomato Warning began with a mistake made by a three-year-old boy, and it became a brand because his father couldn't
> stop thinking about what that mistake meant.

> **The Moment.**

> Summer. Southern Illinois. The part of the country where tornado season is not an abstraction -- it is a calendar
> event, as reliable and as feared as tax day. The family was inside. A tornado warning had appeared on the television,
> that familiar crawl of text across the bottom of the screen that means: pay attention, something dangerous is forming,
> you may need to act.

> Max was three years old. He was doing what three-year-olds do during storms -- existing in that space between
> obliviousness and absorption where the adult world's urgency registers as background texture. He looked at the screen.
> He looked at his dad. And he said, with the unshakable confidence that only a child possesses: *"Daddy, there's a
Tomato Warning."*

> His dad laughed. His mom laughed. The storm passed without incident, as most storms do. The phrase entered the family
> lexicon -- one of those private jokes that families carry like heirlooms. "Tomato Warning" became shorthand for the
> absurd, the innocent, the moment when a child accidentally says something more interesting than what was actually
> there.

> **The Move.**

> The family moved to Florida shortly after. New state, new chapter. But the weather has a way of following you, or more
> precisely, Florida has a way of teaching you that weather is never just weather. It is infrastructure. It is
> economics.
> It is the difference between a community that exists on Tuesday and one that is fundamentally altered by Wednesday.

> Gulf Coast hurricanes became the new normal. Not the distant, televised kind -- the kind where you board windows, fill
> bathtubs, charge devices, and wait. The kind where the morning after smells like wet insulation and gasoline. The kind
> where neighbors you've never spoken to suddenly become the most important people in your life because they have a
> generator and you do not.

> The family watched storms reshape the geography of their community. They watched the aftermath in eastern Tennessee,
> in western North Carolina -- places where the word "unprecedented" stopped being a news headline and started being a
> lived experience. They saw what happens in the gap between the disaster and the recovery: volunteers filling trucks
> with
> water, nonprofits coordinating shelter logistics, ordinary people doing extraordinary work because the systems
> designed
> to help were overwhelmed.

> **The Name.**

> At some point -- and the exact moment is impossible to pin down, because ideas like this don't arrive with
> timestamps -- the father looked at the growing collection of hot sauces in his kitchen, thought about the storms that
> had shaped his family's life, and heard his son's voice: *Tomato Warning.*

> Not as a joke anymore. As a name.

> The Enhanced Fujita scale -- the system meteorologists use to rate tornado intensity from EF-1 (minor damage) to
> EF-5 (total destruction) -- became a heat scale. Five salsas, five storms, each one escalating in intensity and
> character. Florida tomatoes. Gulf Coast flavors. Peppers sourced for their specific heat signatures, not just their
> Scoville numbers. Every jar designed to be a conversation about flavor first, heat second, and purpose always.

> Because if storms were going to be the through-line of this family's life -- from a child's mispronunciation in
> Illinois to hurricane shutters in Florida -- then storms would also be the through-line of this brand. And if the
> brand
> borrowed the language of destruction, it would pay that debt by funding reconstruction.

> **The Mission.**

> At least 10% of Tomato Warning's net profits go to disaster relief. Not a marketing gesture. A structural commitment.
> Named charity partners. Public annual reporting. Dollars that move from a jar on your table to a family rebuilding
> after
> the storm.

> The fundraising channel extends this further: schools, scout troops, sports teams, and community organizations sell
> Tomato Warning salsas to raise money for their own programs. Premium product. Generous margins. A story that sells
> itself.

> **The Truth.**

> Tomato Warning is what happens when a family takes the most ordinary, most human moment imaginable -- a child getting
> a word wrong -- and refuses to let it stay ordinary. It is a brand built on the belief that the best things in life
> are
> accidents that you choose to take seriously. That flavor and philanthropy are not opposing forces. That a jar of salsa
> can carry a story worth telling and a mission worth funding.

> A three-year-old heard a warning and turned it into something beautiful. His family is building the rest.

## 2. Brand Archetype

### Primary Archetype: The Creator

Tomato Warning builds something from nothing. It takes raw materials -- tomatoes, peppers, spices -- and transforms them
into experiences. It takes a child's mispronunciation and transforms it into a brand. It takes the destructive power of
storms and transforms it into a force for recovery. The Creator archetype is about vision, craft, and the belief that
what you make can change the world.

### Secondary Archetype: The Caregiver

The mission is not a marketing strategy. It is the reason the brand exists. The Caregiver archetype shows up in the 10%
commitment, in the fundraising channel, in the refusal to treat disaster relief as a PR opportunity. The Caregiver in
Tomato Warning is not soft or sentimental -- it is practical, structural, and accountable.

### Shadow Archetype: The Outlaw (contained)

There is a rebellious streak in this brand. Rating salsa on the tornado scale. Naming your hottest product "Ground
Zero." Using the language of destruction to sell condiments. This is playful defiance -- the kind that makes people grin
rather than flinch. The Outlaw energy must be contained and intentional: it lives in the product names, the heat scale,
the irreverent product copy. It never touches the mission.

### The Archetype in Practice

- **When we talk about FLAVOR:** Creator energy. Craft. Intention. "We built this."
- **When we talk about HEAT:** Outlaw energy. Challenge. Grin. "We dare you."
- **When we talk about MISSION:** Caregiver energy. Earnest. Accountable. "We help."
- **When we talk about ORIGIN:** All three. A creative act (Creator) born from defiance of the expected (Outlaw) driven
  by the desire to help (Caregiver).

## 3. The Brand's Narrative World

Every great brand lives in a world. Nike lives in the world of athletic struggle and triumph. Patagonia lives in the
wild places that need protecting. Tomato Warning lives in the world of weather -- specifically, in the dramatic space
between the calm before the storm and the rebuilding after it.

### The Storm as Metaphor

The storm is not just our theme. It is our organizing principle. Everything in this brand maps to weather:

| Brand Element     | Weather Equivalent                                              |
|-------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------|
| Product line      | The EF scale (intensity spectrum)                               |
| Flavor experience | The storm itself (building, arriving, devastating)              |
| Heat level        | Wind speed (measurable, honest, scientific)                     |
| Origin story      | The first warning (innocence meeting force)                     |
| Mission           | Recovery after the storm (rebuilding, community)                |
| Customer journey  | Chasing storms (exploration, thrill, respect)                   |
| Brand growth      | The storm system expanding (from local to regional to national) |

### The Three Settings

**1. The Kitchen Table**
This is where the salsa lives -- where flavor happens, where families gather, where the product does its primary work.
The kitchen table is warm, communal, alive. It is the "sunshine" to the storm's darkness. Tomato Warning exists to be ON
the table, not just in the pantry. The kitchen table is the destination.

**2. The Storm**
This is the brand's dramatic engine. The storm is exciting, dangerous, beautiful, and destructive. It provides our
vocabulary (EF ratings, storm names, weather language), our visual identity (radar imagery, storm colors, pressure
maps), and our emotional range (from calm breezes to total devastation). The storm is the story.

**3. The Aftermath**
This is where the mission lives. After the storm, there is damage, and there is recovery. There are communities that
need help and people who show up to provide it. The aftermath is where Tomato Warning's purpose becomes real -- where
the brand stops being about salsa and starts being about people. The aftermath is the meaning.

### The EF Scale as Journey

The five products are not just a lineup. They are a narrative arc -- a hero's journey told in peppers:

- **EF-1 (Coastal Calm):** The ordinary world. Comfortable. Beautiful. The place you start.
- **EF-2 (Gulf Breeze):** The call to adventure. Something is building. You feel the shift.
- **EF-3 (Squall Line):** Crossing the threshold. The storm arrives. There is no going back.
- **EF-4 (Supercell):** The ordeal. Deep, relentless, demanding respect. Transformation happens here.
- **EF-5 (Ground Zero):** The ultimate test. Total commitment. Only the dedicated arrive here, and they are changed.

This journey mirrors both the literal progression of a storm system AND the customer's exploration of the brand. Most
people enter at EF-1 or EF-2. Some climb the scale. A few reach EF-5. Each step is a story.

## 4. Product Narratives

Each product is a character in the Tomato Warning story. Below, the narrative architecture for each.

### EF-1: Coastal Calm

**The Character:** The golden hour before the atmosphere shifts. Coastal Calm is beauty, brightness, and the kind of
quiet confidence that doesn't need to prove anything. It is the friend who brings the best dish to the cookout -- no
drama, just excellence.

**The Moment:** Sunday morning. Eggs on the skillet. The window is open. There might be weather later, but right now,
there is sunlight and the smell of fresh cilantro and lime. This is the salsa that lives in that moment.

**The Audience:** Everyone. Coastal Calm is the gateway, the handshake, the first impression. It is for the person who
says "I don't really do spicy" and the person who loves heat but also loves flavor without fire. It is the everyday
salsa for people who refuse to eat boring food.

**The Emotional Truth:** Calm is not the absence of power. It is power at rest.

**Ingredients as Story:** Sun-ripened Florida tomatoes, crisp poblano peppers, bright cilantro, and fresh lime. Every
ingredient is visible, honest, recognizable. You could name them all by tasting it. 500-1,500 SHU -- a whisper of
warmth, nothing more.

---

### EF-2: Gulf Breeze

**The Character:** The barometer dropping. The first signs that the atmosphere is organizing into something purposeful.
Gulf Breeze is anticipation made edible -- the deep, smoky promise that something bigger is building.

**The Moment:** Late afternoon, backyard grill. The coals are white-hot. You can smell the chipotle before you taste it.
The first bite has depth -- real depth, the kind that makes you pause and pay attention. Something is happening.

**The Audience:** The curious. The people who ordered medium their whole lives and are ready to admit they want more.
Gulf Breeze is the product that teaches customers they are braver than they thought.

**The Emotional Truth:** The most exciting moment in any storm is not the peak -- it is the building. That is where Gulf
Breeze lives.

**Ingredients as Story:** Roasted tomatoes, chipotle peppers that carry the memory of smoke, sharp serrano peppers for
the first real strike of heat, and apple cider vinegar for brightness that cuts through the darkness. 1,500-5,000 SHU --
you will notice this. You will want to.

---

### EF-3: Squall Line

**The Character:** The collision. The moment warm air meets cold air and the atmosphere cracks open. Squall Line is a
controlled explosion of contrast -- tropical sweetness and genuine heat occupying the same bite, refusing to cancel each
other out.

**The Moment:** Friday night. The kitchen is loud with friends. Someone dips a chip and their eyes go wide -- not from
pain, but from surprise. "What IS this?" That is the Squall Line moment. Surprise. Delight. A second dip before the
first has finished.

**The Audience:** The adventurer. Squall Line is for the person who orders the dish the server warns them about. The
person who treats food as experience, not sustenance. This is the product that earns the most social media shares
because it is the most fun to watch someone try for the first time.

**The Emotional Truth:** The best storms are beautiful and dangerous at the same time. So is this salsa.

**Ingredients as Story:** Pure habanero heat -- direct, honest, inescapable -- countered by golden ripe mango that
refuses to let the fire have the last word. Most habanero-mango salsas blend the two flavors into a single note. Squall
Line sequences them: sweet, then heat, like a weather system rolling through. You taste the mango first. Then the
habanero arrives. This is not a mild salsa with a novelty pepper. This is a real storm. 5,000-25,000 SHU.

---

### EF-4: Supercell

**The Character:** The organized monster. A supercell is the rarest, most powerful type of thunderstorm -- a rotating
column of atmospheric fury that meteorologists track with reverence. This salsa demands the same respect. It is not
trying to hurt you. It is simply operating at a level that requires your full attention.

**The Moment:** The quiet dinner. Just you and something you grilled with intention. Maybe a ribeye, maybe short ribs.
You add Supercell not for the heat -- you are past proving anything to anyone -- but for the depth. Ghost pepper and
black garlic create something that doesn't exist without both of them: dark, savory, relentless, rewarding. This is the
salsa equivalent of a rare whiskey.

**The Audience:** The committed. Supercell is not for beginners and it does not pretend to be. It is for the person who
has been climbing the EF scale and is ready for the penultimate challenge. It is also for the person who skips directly
here because they know what they like and they like intensity.

**The Emotional Truth:** Real power does not announce itself. It simply arrives.

**Ingredients as Story:** Ghost pepper dictates the terms. Black garlic provides umami depth -- a fermented, almost
sweet darkness that grounds the heat in something substantial. Red fresno peppers add a bright, sharp counterpoint.
25,000-100,000 SHU. This is not casual. This is deliberate.

---

### EF-5: Ground Zero

**The Character:** The end of the scale. Ground Zero is not a dare, a gimmick, or a novelty. It is the logical
conclusion of a brand that takes heat seriously. When you rate your products on the tornado scale, EF-5 must exist, and
it must be real.

**The Moment:** The moment is not about food. The moment is about the person. Ground Zero finds its audience in the
people who look at a Carolina Reaper and see a challenge worth accepting -- not because they want to suffer, but because
they want to know what exists at the edge. This is the mountaineer's "because it's there."

**The Audience:** The few. Ground Zero is deliberately exclusionary -- and that exclusivity is its marketing. Every
person who can't handle it becomes an evangelist: "You have to try EF-5." Every person who can becomes a loyalist. The
audience is small and fierce.

**The Emotional Truth:** Total devastation is not the absence of flavor. It is flavor at its most extreme -- so intense
that everything else falls away.

**Ingredients as Story:** Carolina Reaper and Trinidad Moruga Scorpion -- the two hottest cultivated peppers on earth --
drive this blend. A charred tomato base provides the only structure left standing. 100,000-500,000+ SHU. This is Ground
Zero. You were warned.

## 5. The Mission Narrative

This is the hardest section to write, and the most important. The connection between a jar of salsa and a family
recovering from a hurricane must be drawn with absolute honesty. The moment it feels manipulative, we lose the audience
we most need -- the Jennifers of the world, the conscious buyers who will scrutinize our giving structure and walk away
if it smells performative.

### The Principle

**We earned the right to this mission. We did not choose it for marketing purposes.**

The origin story is not "we saw an opportunity in cause marketing." The origin story is "storms have been the defining
experience of this family's life -- from an Illinois tornado warning to Gulf Coast hurricanes -- and building a brand
around storms without helping people survive them would be dishonest."

That distinction matters. It is the difference between authenticity and performance.

### How We Tell the Mission Story

**Lead with the connection, not the numbers.**
Wrong: "We donate 10% of net profits to disaster relief."
Right: "A three-year-old heard a storm warning and turned it into a name. Now that name helps communities rebuild after
the real thing."

The numbers matter -- and we share them, publicly, annually, with specifics. But the numbers are proof. The story is the
point.

**Name the partners. Show the work.**
We do not say "a portion of proceeds goes to disaster relief." We name our charity partners. We report actual dollar
amounts. We show where the money went and what it did. Transparency is not optional. In an era of cause-marketing
skepticism, vague language is the same as lying.

**Never exploit tragedy.**
We can reference the reality of disaster without turning victims into props. The mission narrative should always center
the helpers -- the organizations, the volunteers, the communities that rebuild. We stand with them. We fund them. We do
not use their pain to sell salsa.

**Separate the product tone from the mission tone.**
The product can be playful, bold, irreverent. "Total devastation -- for the few who dare" works for salsa copy. It does
NOT work when we are talking about actual storms that destroyed actual homes. The voice shifts when the subject shifts.
Always.

### The Mission in Three Sentences

*For when you need the mission fast and clean:*

> Storms named this brand. Storms drive its mission. At least 10% of Tomato Warning's net profits go to disaster relief
> organizations helping communities prepare for, survive, and recover from extreme weather.

### The Fundraising Story

The fundraising channel is a second expression of the mission -- not disaster relief, but community investment. Schools,
scout troops, sports teams, and local organizations sell Tomato Warning at generous margins to fund their own programs.

This story is simpler and lighter:

> Tomato Warning salsa helps communities in two ways: our profits fund disaster relief, and our fundraising program
> helps local organizations raise money for the programs that matter to them. Premium product. Great margins. A story
> that
> practically sells itself.

Coach Dave (our fundraiser persona) does not need the full origin myth. He needs to know: the product is good, the
margins are strong, the story helps him pitch parents, and the shelf life means no logistics headaches.

### The Pre-Profit Narrative

In Year 1, net profits may be zero or negative. This creates a critical storytelling challenge: how do we tell the
mission story before there are profits to donate?

**What we can say:** "Our mission is to donate at least 10% of net profits to disaster relief organizations. As a new
brand, we are building toward that commitment and will publish our first impact report in [specific quarter]." We can
also highlight the fundraising partner channel, which operates independently of profits -- providing product at or below
cost to community organizations.

**What we cannot say:** "Every jar helps disaster relief" (not true until profitable). "A portion of your purchase goes
to charity" (misleading if no profits yet). Any language that implies current, active giving before giving has actually
occurred.

The pre-profit period is where trust is either built or broken. Honesty about our scale and timeline is more valuable
than premature mission claims. (See Content Strategy, Section 7 for detailed pre-profit communication guidelines.)

## 6. Audience Story Mapping

Each persona enters the Tomato Warning story at a different point, looking for a different thing, and experiencing a
different transformation. The brand's job is to meet each person where they are.

### Marcus, 34 -- "The Flavor Chaser"

**His chapter:** The Discovery. Marcus is always looking for the next great food experience. He enters the Tomato
Warning story the way he enters every brand story -- through the product. He sees the EF scale and he is intrigued. He
tries EF-3 because he wants the interesting one. He discovers the origin story on the jar and it makes him smile. He
posts about it.

**His transformation:** From consumer to evangelist. Marcus does not just buy Tomato Warning. He tells people about it.
The EF scale gives him a vocabulary ("I'm an EF-4 guy"), and the origin story gives him a narrative ("You have to hear
how this brand started"). He becomes a storyteller for the brand without being asked to.

**His emotional journey:** Curiosity (what is this?) --> Surprise (this is really good) --> Identity (this is my
brand) --> Advocacy (you need to try this).

**Best channels to reach Marcus:** Instagram, food creator partnerships, specialty grocery placement, tasting events.

---

### Jennifer, 42 -- "The Conscious Buyer"

**Her chapter:** The Investigation. Jennifer does not impulse-buy. She evaluates. She enters the Tomato Warning story
the way she enters every brand relationship -- by checking if the values are real. She will read the back of the jar.
She will visit the website. She will look for the named charity partners and the annual giving reports.

**Her transformation:** From skeptic to advocate. If Jennifer trusts Tomato Warning, she does not just buy it -- she
recruits for it. She tells her book club, her neighbors, her nonprofit colleagues. She buys gift sets for people she
respects. But earning her trust requires structural honesty, not marketing language.

**Her emotional journey:** Interest (this looks premium) --> Scrutiny (is the mission real?) --> Validation (they named
partners, they report numbers) --> Trust (I believe this brand) --> Loyalty (I will choose this every time).

**Best channels to reach Jennifer:** The mission page on the website. Press features that explore the giving structure.
Word of mouth from other Jennifers. In-store presence at Publix and specialty grocers she already trusts.

---

### Coach Dave, 48 -- "The Fundraiser Organizer"

**His chapter:** The Solution. Dave does not care about brand stories. He cares about raising $1,000 for his soccer team
without spending his weekend managing frozen cookie dough. He enters the Tomato Warning story through the fundraising
channel -- and the story he needs is simple: this product is good, it is easy to sell, the margins are strong, and there
is a built-in pitch (disaster relief + premium salsa).

**His transformation:** From skeptic to repeat partner. If the first fundraiser works -- if parents buy enthusiastically
and the check arrives on time -- Dave becomes a repeat customer and a referral source. He tells other coaches. He tells
the PTA. He does not need to love the brand's mythology. He needs the program to deliver.

**His emotional journey:** Skepticism (another fundraiser product?) --> Curiosity (salsa is different) --> Trial (let's
try one season) --> Relief (this actually worked) --> Loyalty (we're doing this every year).

**Best channels to reach Dave:** Direct outreach to youth sports organizations. Fundraising program landing page.
Testimonials from other coaches. In-person demos at coaching conferences or school events.

## 7. Story Adaptation Guidelines

The core narrative must flex across channels and formats without losing its essential truth. Below, guidelines for each
medium.

### Packaging (Jar Labels)

- **Front label:** Product name, EF rating, and heat descriptor only. The front is about the product, not the story.
- **Back label:** The 15-second origin story, charity commitment, QR code linking to the full story and impact reports.
  The back is where the story lives.
- **Tone:** Confident, concise, premium. No exclamation points. No "Hey there!" casualness. The label speaks like a
  weather report: authoritative and clear.

### Social Media

- **Origin story content:** Use the 15-second version. Pair with the "Tomato Warning vs. Tornado Warning" moment. Video
  of a kid mispronouncing words is universally shareable.
- **Product content:** Each EF level gets its own visual identity and voice. EF-1 content is warm and inviting. EF-5
  content is dark, minimal, and daring.
- **Mission content:** Share impact reports, charity partner spotlights, and community recovery stories. Never show
  disaster imagery for engagement. Show rebuilding, not destruction.
- **Tone shifts by EF level:**
    - EF-1/EF-2: Warm, approachable, inviting
    - EF-3: Bold, vibrant, exciting
    - EF-4/EF-5: Intense, minimal, respect-commanding

### Email

- **Welcome sequence:** Tell the origin story across 3-4 emails. Email 1: the mispronunciation. Email 2: the products
  and the EF scale. Email 3: the mission. Email 4: find your EF level (product recommendation).
- **Ongoing:** Mix product features, recipes/pairings, mission updates, and community spotlights. The email voice is
  slightly warmer and more personal than social -- like a letter from someone you trust.

### Press / Media

- Use the 3-minute version as the press narrative foundation.
- Lead with the origin story. It is the most shareable, most unusual element.
- Have specific numbers ready: Scoville ranges, giving percentages, named charity partners.
- The EF-5 is the PR hero -- extreme heat + extreme charity = a story journalists want to write.

### In-Person (Farmers Markets, Tastings, Retail Demos)

- The 60-second version is the primary tool.
- Let people taste before you tell the story. Flavor first. Then: "Want to hear how this brand started?"
- The EF scale is a physical experience at a tasting table: watch people's faces change from EF-1 to EF-3. That is the
  story telling itself.
- Have the mission ready but do not lead with it. If people ask "what makes you different?" -- THEN the mission is the
  answer.

### Video

- **Origin story video (flagship content):** Build from the 3-minute version. Open with the Illinois storm. The child's
  voice. The move to Florida. The hurricanes. The decision. End with the product lineup and the mission. This should
  feel like a short film, not a commercial.
- **Product videos:** Each EF level gets its own visual treatment. EF-1 is bright and sun-washed. EF-5 is dark, smoky,
  dramatic. The visual progression mirrors the heat progression.
- **Mission videos:** Follow the money. Show the charity partners. Show the work they do. Interview the people, not the
  brand. Let the impact speak.

## 8. The Stories We Never Tell

Every brand needs boundaries. These are ours. Non-negotiable.

### We never exploit real disaster victims.

We can reference hurricanes, tornadoes, and severe weather. We can talk about the destruction they cause and the
communities they affect. We CANNOT use images, stories, or details of specific victims or specific tragedies to sell
product. Our mission funds recovery. Our marketing does not capitalize on suffering.

### We never make light of actual destruction.

"Total devastation" works as a heat descriptor for EF-5. It does NOT work as a caption on a post during an active
hurricane. We pause all promotional content during major weather events affecting our communities. We can post about our
relief efforts. We cannot sell during someone else's emergency.

### We never overstate our impact.

If we donated $5,000 last year, we say we donated $5,000. We do not say "we're changing the world" or "every jar saves
lives." We are honest about our scale. We are a small brand doing meaningful work at our current capacity. Overpromising
is the fastest way to become the kind of brand Jennifer walks away from.

### We never blur the line between product playfulness and mission seriousness.

The EF-scale names can be fun. The storm language can be bold. The product copy can be irreverent. But the MOMENT we are
talking about real storms, real damage, real people -- the tone shifts. There is no joke in the mission section. There
is no winking reference to destruction when we are funding rebuilding. These two voices coexist in the brand, but they
do not cross.

### We never claim the mission is the reason to buy.

The reason to buy is the salsa. The flavor, the quality, the ingredients, the craft. The mission is the reason to feel
good about buying. The mission is the reason to stay loyal. But if the salsa is not exceptional, the mission does not
matter. Product quality is the foundation. Everything else is built on top of it.

### We never use "charity" as a shield from criticism.

If someone says our salsa is too salty, the answer is not "but we donate to disaster relief." Product criticism gets
product responses. Mission criticism gets mission responses. They are separate conversations and conflating them is
dishonest.

### We acknowledge "Ground Zero" carries secondary associations.

The EF-5 product name "Ground Zero" is a meteorological term describing the point of greatest impact. It is also
permanently associated with the September 11 attacks. We use the name in its storm context, but we must be prepared for
the association to be raised publicly. When it is, we respond with empathy and transparency: the name comes from weather
science, and we understand why it resonates differently for some people. We never dismiss the concern. The Brand Council
should periodically evaluate whether the naming risk outweighs the branding value, and a pre-prepared, empathetic
response should exist before launch.

### The Screenshot Test

Before publishing any content -- especially content using storm language, destruction metaphors, or charitable claims --
apply the Screenshot Test (defined in detail in the Content Strategy, Section 9.3):

> If this single piece of content were screenshot'd, removed from all context, and shared by someone hostile to the
> brand, would it make us look bad?

For a storm-themed brand that donates to disaster relief, this test is not optional. It is existential. Every piece of
content must pass it.

### Content Pause Protocol

During a named storm or major weather event affecting our operating regions:

1. **Immediately pause** all scheduled promotional content.
2. **Assess** whether to post about our relief efforts (only if we are actively providing support).
3. **Resume** promotional content only after the immediate crisis has passed and recovery is underway.
4. **Never** reference the specific event in future product marketing unless it is a genuine mission update about our
   relief contribution.

For detailed operational protocols including timing, authority, and pivot messaging, see the Content Strategy (Section
9.1 and 9.2).
