# Brand Voice & Tone Guide

*The constitution for how Tomato Warning speaks, everywhere.*

---

## The Golden Rule

**The product is playful; the mission is earnest. Never mix them up.**

This is the single most important sentence in our entire brand. It governs every word we write, every caption we post,
every pitch we make.

When we talk about **flavor, heat, the EF scale, food pairings, product experiences** — we are bold, clever,
storm-charged, and confident. We have fun. We lean into the weather metaphors. We dare people to try EF-5.

When we talk about **disaster relief, community recovery, the families we help, the donations we make** — the cleverness
stops. We are direct, specific, warm, and credible. We name the organizations. We cite the numbers. We honor the reality
that storms destroy lives.

**Why this matters:** Our brand exists at the intersection of fun and purpose. If we let the playfulness bleed into the
mission, we look like we're trivializing real suffering to sell salsa. If we let the earnestness flatten the product
voice, we become a charity that happens to make hot sauce — and nobody buys that jar off the shelf. Both sides need to
be strong. Neither side gets to colonize the other.

### The Golden Rule in Practice

| Context                         | Right                                                                                                                                 | Wrong                                                                                          |
|---------------------------------|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Product page for EF-4 Supercell | "Rotation confirmed. Ghost pepper dictates the heat. Black garlic grounds the flavor in pure, savory darkness."                       | "Buy Supercell and help disaster victims recover from devastating storms."                     |
| Donation announcement           | "This quarter, Tomato Warning donated $12,400 to Direct Relief for Hurricane Milton recovery in Lee County, Florida."                 | "We STORMED into hurricane season with a category-5 donation! Total devastation... of hunger!" |
| Social post about a new flavor  | "EF-3 Squall Line just dropped. Habanero-forward. Mango reprieve. The storm is here."                                                 | "Every jar of Squall Line helps tornado survivors rebuild their shattered lives."              |
| Fundraiser partner pitch        | "Your organization keeps 50% of every sale. The product sells itself — premium salsa at a price point families are happy to support." | "Help your team weather the fundraising storm with our EF-scale salsa lineup!"                 |

The test: **Would a person who just lost their home in a tornado feel respected by this sentence?** If the answer is no,
or even maybe, rewrite it.

## Brand Voice Principles

Our voice has five core attributes. These are constant — they don't change based on context. What changes is the
*tone* (see Tone Spectrum below), which adjusts how much of each attribute comes forward.

### 1. Boldly Confident

We know what we are and we're not apologetic about it. We make premium salsa rated on the tornado scale. That's unusual,
and we own it without over-explaining or hedging. We don't ask for permission. We don't use weak qualifiers. We state
things.

- **Sounds like:** "Five salsas. One storm scale. No compromises."
- **Doesn't sound like:** "We think you might really enjoy our unique take on the salsa experience!"

### 2. Flavor-Obsessed

Every conversation about our product comes back to what's in the jar. We talk about ingredients with specificity — not "
premium peppers" but "ghost pepper and black garlic." We talk about taste with authority — not "it's pretty hot" but "
rotation confirmed — deep, relentless, rewarding." Flavor is our credibility. Heat is just one dimension of it.

- **Sounds like:** "Sun-ripened Florida tomatoes. Crisp poblano. Bright lime. A flawless, breezy finish."
- **Doesn't sound like:** "Our salsa is made with only the finest, hand-selected ingredients for a truly elevated taste
  experience."

### 3. Storm-Charged (With Restraint)

The weather metaphor is our signature — it's woven into our name, our scale, our product stories, our origin. We use it
with intention and craft. A well-placed storm reference creates atmosphere and memorability. An overused one becomes a
gimmick. The metaphor earns its place when it illuminates the product or the brand's identity. It loses its place when
it's just filling space.

- **Sounds like:** "The barometer drops. The sky darkens. Gulf Breeze introduces the first true signals of atmospheric
  instability."
- **Doesn't sound like:** "This STORMY salsa will BLOW you away with a TORNADO of flavor that will leave your taste buds
  DEVASTATED!"

### 4. Genuinely Purposeful

Our mission isn't a marketing tactic bolted onto a product. It's the reason this company exists. When we talk about what
we give and who we help, we are specific, transparent, and grounded. We name the organizations. We share the amounts. We
describe the impact in concrete terms. We never, ever use disaster as a sales hook.

- **Sounds like:** "10% of net profits go directly to regional disaster relief organizations. This quarter: $12,400 to
  Direct Relief for Hurricane Milton recovery."
- **Doesn't sound like:** "A portion of proceeds goes to help those in need. Together, we can make a difference!"

### 5. Approachably Premium

We are a premium brand — in ingredients, packaging, pricing, and presentation. But premium doesn't mean pretentious.
We're the brand you bring to a cookout, not to a gala. Our sophistication comes from the quality of what we make and the
clarity of how we communicate, not from exclusivity or elitism. We want everyone at the table.

- **Sounds like:** "Premium salsa for real meals. From Tuesday night tacos to Saturday's slow-smoked brisket."
- **Doesn't sound like:** "An artisanal, curated culinary experience crafted for the discerning palate."

## Tone Spectrum

The voice stays constant. The tone adjusts based on where we're speaking and who we're speaking to. Think of it as a
mixing board — all five voice attributes are always present, but we raise and lower the levels depending on context.

### Product Pages & Descriptions

**Dominant attributes:** Storm-Charged, Flavor-Obsessed, Boldly Confident

This is where we have the most fun. Product pages are atmospheric, immersive, and sensory. Every product has a storm
narrative. We write like we're describing weather events, not condiments. The language is vivid but controlled —
evocative, not breathless.

**Example (EF-3 Squall Line):**
> A sudden, violent shift in the atmosphere. Squall Line delivers a rapid, striking wave of pure habanero heat, followed
> immediately by the golden, tropical reprieve of ripe mango. It is a calculated, high-contrast collision of extreme
> weather and deep coastal flavor.

**Guidelines:**

- Lead with the storm metaphor, then ground it in specific flavor details
- Name the peppers and key ingredients — specificity builds credibility
- Keep paragraphs tight: 2-3 sentences maximum
- The heat descriptor is a one-liner, not a paragraph
- Pairings should be specific and appetizing, not generic ("Grilled mahi-mahi tacos" not "Mexican food")

### Mission & Impact Content

**Dominant attributes:** Genuinely Purposeful, Approachably Premium

This is where we earn trust. Mission content is warm, direct, and specific. No storm metaphors when discussing real
disaster impact. No cleverness when describing real suffering. We tell the truth plainly: who we helped, how much we
gave, what it was used for. We let the facts carry the emotional weight.

**Example:**
> When Hurricane Milton made landfall in October 2024, it devastated communities across Lee and Charlotte counties.
> Within 72 hours, our partner Direct Relief had delivered medical supplies to three shelters in Fort Myers. Tomato
> Warning's Q4 donation of $8,200 helped fund that response.

**Guidelines:**

- Name the disaster, the location, the organization, and the dollar amount
- Use plain language — no jargon, no euphemisms, no storm puns
- Describe impact in human terms when possible: families served, meals delivered, supplies distributed
- It's okay to express gratitude to customers for making it possible — just be sincere, not saccharine
- Never use mission content as a sales pitch. The mission stands on its own.

### Social Media

**Dominant attributes:** Boldly Confident, Approachably Premium, Storm-Charged (light touch)

Social is conversational, community-building, and confident without being loud. We talk to our audience like they're
already in the club. Short sentences. Direct address. Personality over perfection. We're not a brand shouting into the
void — we're a neighbor who makes incredible salsa and happens to care about the same things you do.

**Example:**
> EF-3 Squall Line just dropped. Habanero-forward. Mango reprieve. The storm is here. Find your level.

**Example (community engagement):**
> You made it to EF-4? Respect. Tell us what you paired it with.

**Guidelines:**

- Sentence fragments are fine. Lists are fine. Brevity is a virtue.
- Ask questions. Invite engagement. Celebrate customer posts.
- Storm references should be light — a touch of atmosphere, not a full weather report
- Never be snarky, dismissive, or condescending. Confidence is not arrogance.
- Hashtags: use sparingly and intentionally. #TomatoWarning #ChaseTheStorm #FindYourLevel
- Emojis: minimal. One per post maximum, if any.

### Press, Retail & Business Communications

**Dominant attributes:** Boldly Confident, Approachably Premium, Genuinely Purposeful

When talking to press, retail buyers, or business partners, we're professional but not corporate. We lead with what
makes us different (the EF scale, the mission, the origin story) and back it up with data and specifics. We don't beg
for attention — we present a compelling brand with a clear value proposition.

**Example (retail pitch):**
> Tomato Warning is a premium salsa brand rated on the Enhanced Fujita tornado scale — five products from
> 500 SHU to 500,000+ SHU. The EF rating system solves a real consumer problem: inconsistent heat labeling across the
> category. 10% of net profits fund regional disaster relief. The brand launched DTC in Gulf Coast Florida in 2026.

**Guidelines:**

- Lead with the differentiation, not the backstory
- Include relevant data: Scoville ranges, profit percentages, market positioning
- Origin story is a supporting detail, not the opening — save it for the second paragraph
- Never sound desperate, overly casual, or like we're reading from a script
- Professional warmth, not corporate formality

### Fundraiser & Partner Outreach

**Dominant attributes:** Genuinely Purposeful, Approachably Premium

Fundraiser outreach is warm, practical, and partnership-oriented. We're talking to coaches, teachers, nonprofit
organizers, and community leaders. They need to know three things: the product is high-quality, the margins are strong,
and the mission is real. We make it easy for them to say yes.

**Example:**
> Your organization keeps 50% of every sale — no upfront cost, no minimum order, no freezer logistics. Tomato Warning is
> premium, shelf-stable salsa that families are excited to buy and keep buying. And every jar sold helps fund disaster
> relief in communities like yours.

**Guidelines:**

- Lead with the practical benefits: margin percentage, no upfront cost, shelf stability
- Mention the mission, but don't oversell it — let the product and the terms do the work
- Keep it simple. Organizers are busy. Bullet points and clear numbers over paragraphs.
- Offer custom labeling as a value-add, not a core pitch
- Follow up is friendly and patient, never pushy

## Vocabulary Bible

| EF-1 through EF-5             | Always hyphenated. Always followed by the product name on first reference (e.g., "EF-3 Squall Line"). Subsequent references can use just the EF number. |
| Enhanced Fujita scale         | Full name on first reference in formal contexts. "EF scale" is fine for casual use.                                                                     |
| Scoville / SHU                | Use when providing specific heat data. Builds credibility with heat enthusiasts.                                                                        |
| Premium                       | Appropriate when describing our positioning, ingredients, or packaging. Not every sentence.                                                             |
| Storm-themed / storm-inspired | Accurate descriptors for external/press contexts. Internally, we just *are* the storm.                                                                  |
| Chase the storm               | Primary tagline. Use as-is, no modifications.                                                                                                           |

| Brace yourself                | Secondary tagline. Use sparingly — it's punchy but can feel aggressive in the wrong context.                                                            |

### Banned Terms

| Term                                   | Why                                                                                                                               |
|----------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| "Portion of proceeds"                  | Vague. We are specific about our giving.                                                                                          |
| "Those in need" / "the less fortunate" | Othering language. Name the communities, the events, the organizations.                                                           |
| "Make a difference"                    | Empty. Show the difference with facts.                                                                                            |
| "Artisanal"                            | Overused to the point of meaninglessness. We say "small-batch" or "premium."                                                      |
| "Curated"                              | We don't curate salsa. We make it.                                                                                                |
| "Elevated"                             | Pretentious. Our quality speaks for itself.                                                                                       |
| "Journey" (as brand metaphor)          | We don't go on journeys. We weather storms.                                                                                       |
| "Family recipe"                        | It's not. Don't pretend it is.                                                                                                    |
| "World's best" / "best ever"           | Unsubstantiated superlatives. Let the product earn its reputation.                                                                |
| "Guilt-free"                           | We're not in the guilt business. Our product is premium salsa that funds disaster relief. Neither of those things involves guilt. |
| "Game-changer" / "disruptor"           | Startup jargon. We make salsa.                                                                                                    |
| Any competitor brand name              | Never reference competitors in our communications.                                                                                |

### Storm Metaphor Usage

Storm metaphors are the heart of our brand language. They're also the easiest thing to get wrong. Here's the framework:

**Use storm metaphors when:**

- Describing products, flavors, and the EF scale
- Writing product stories and packaging copy
- Creating atmosphere on the website and in marketing materials
- Describing the brand's identity and origin ("Born in a storm")
- Social media product posts

**Never use storm metaphors when:**

- Discussing specific real disasters or their aftermath
- Describing the suffering of real people affected by storms
- Writing donation announcements or impact reports
- Communicating during an active weather emergency in our market area
- Responding to news about storm damage or loss of life

**Use storm metaphors carefully when:**

- Fundraiser outreach (a light touch is okay — "Join the Storm" — but never as the dominant frame)
- Press communications (one or two is atmospheric; five is a weather report)
- Internal communications (keep it natural, not forced)

**The litmus test:** If a headline about a real tornado appeared next to your copy, would it still feel appropriate? If
not, dial it back.

## Writing Style Rules

### Sentence Structure

- **Vary sentence length deliberately.** Short sentences create impact. Longer sentences build atmosphere and draw the
  reader through a sensory experience. Alternate between the two.
- **Sentence fragments are acceptable** in product copy, social media, and taglines. Not in mission content, press
  communications, or formal business writing.
- **Active voice always.** "We donated $12,400" not "A donation of $12,400 was made."

### Paragraph Structure

- **Product copy:** 2-3 sentences per paragraph maximum. Dense and atmospheric.
- **Mission content:** 3-4 sentences per paragraph. Clear and grounded.
- **Web copy:** Short paragraphs. White space is our friend. Scanning is expected.
- **Business documents:** Standard paragraph length is fine. Clarity over brevity.

### Headlines & CTAs

- **Headlines are declarative, not interrogative.** "The storm is here." not "Ready for the storm?"
- **CTAs are direct and confident.** "Find your level." / "Explore the lineup." / "Join the storm."
- **No exclamation points in headlines.** Period. (Literally.)
- **Subheads orient the reader.** They should make sense if you read only the subheads on a page.

### Punctuation

- **Em dashes** — yes, generously. They create drama and pacing in product copy. They also clarify complex sentences in
  business writing.
- **Exclamation points** — almost never. One per page maximum in marketing copy. Zero in mission content and business
  communications. If the sentence needs an exclamation point to feel energetic, the sentence isn't strong enough.
- **Oxford comma** — always. "Tomato, habanero, and mango" not "Tomato, habanero and mango."
- **Ellipses** — very rarely, and only for deliberate trailing effect in product copy. Never in business writing.
- **Semicolons** — acceptable in business writing. Avoid in product copy and social media.

### Numbers & Data

- Spell out one through nine. Use numerals for 10 and above.
- Always use numerals for: Scoville ratings (500 SHU), EF levels (EF-1), dollar amounts ($12,400), and percentages (
  10%).
- In donation reporting, use exact figures when available. Rounded figures are acceptable for projections.

### Capitalization

- **EF-1 through EF-5** — always capitalized, always hyphenated.
- **Product names** — capitalized: Coastal Calm, Gulf Breeze, Squall Line, Supercell, Ground Zero.
- **Tomato Warning** — always two words, both capitalized. Never "TW" in external communications.
- **Enhanced Fujita** — capitalized. It's a proper noun (named after Dr. Ted Fujita).
- **Headline style** — sentence case for subheads and social captions. Title case for product names and formal document
  headers.

## Do's and Don'ts

### Product & Flavor Copy

| Do                                                                                          | Don't                                                                        |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| "Ghost pepper dictates the heat. Black garlic grounds the flavor in pure, savory darkness." | "This amazing salsa is incredibly hot and super flavorful — you'll love it!" |
| "500 to 1,500 SHU. A gentle gust — big flavor, little fire."                                | "Mild heat that everyone can enjoy! Perfect for the whole family!"           |
| "Habanero-forward with tropical mango counter."                                             | "A delicious blend of peppers and fruit for a uniquely elevated taste."      |
| Name the specific peppers, ingredients, and Scoville range.                                 | Use vague descriptors like "premium peppers" or "select ingredients."        |

### Mission & Impact Content

| Do                                                                                               | Don't                                                                       |
|--------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| "This quarter, we donated $12,400 to Direct Relief for Hurricane Milton recovery in Lee County." | "We're proud to give back and make a difference in communities everywhere!" |
| "10% of net profits fund regional disaster relief."                                              | "A portion of our proceeds helps those in need."                            |
| Describe real impact: families served, supplies delivered, organizations funded.                 | Use stock photos of sad-looking disaster scenes to sell salsa.              |
| Let the facts carry the emotional weight.                                                        | Layer on adjectives to manufacture emotion the facts already provide.       |

### Social Media

| Do                                                                | Don't                                                                |
|-------------------------------------------------------------------|----------------------------------------------------------------------|
| "EF-4 Supercell. Ghost pepper. Black garlic. Rotation confirmed." | "OMG you guys THIS salsa is INSANE!! You NEED to try it!!! #blessed" |
| "You made it to EF-3? Respect. What'd you pair it with?"          | "LIKE and SHARE if you love spicy food!!!!"                          |
| One or two relevant hashtags.                                     | A wall of 15 hashtags at the end of every post.                      |
| Respond to customers with warmth and personality.                 | Ignore, delete, or argue with negative feedback.                     |

### Sales & Retail

| Do                                                                        | Don't                                                                                    |
|---------------------------------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| "Five products, 500 to 500,000+ SHU, rated on the Enhanced Fujita scale." | "We're basically the Patagonia of salsa!"                                                |
| Lead with the product differentiation and market data.                    | Lead with the sob story. The mission supports the brand; it doesn't replace the product. |
| "10% of net profits to named disaster relief organizations."              | "We donate to charity."                                                                  |
| Be professional, prepared, and specific.                                  | Be casual, vague, or assume the buyer is already sold.                                   |

## The Voice Test

Before publishing any copy — product page, social post, email, press release, donation report, sales deck, or fundraiser
pitch — run it through these seven checks:

- [ ] **The Golden Rule check:** Is the product voice playful and the mission voice earnest? Are they staying in their
  lanes?
- [ ] **The specificity check:** Have I named the peppers, the Scoville range, the organization, the dollar amount, or
  whatever concrete detail belongs here? Or am I hiding behind vague generalities?
- [ ] **The storm metaphor check:** Are the weather references earning their place, or are they just filling space?
  Would this still read well next to a real storm headline?
- [ ] **The authenticity check:** Does this sound like it came from people who genuinely care about flavor and
  community? Or does it sound like it came from a branding agency?
- [ ] **The confidence check:** Am I stating things or hedging? Am I leading with strength or apologizing for existing?
- [ ] **The pretension check:** Would I say this sentence out loud to a friend at a cookout without feeling ridiculous?
  If not, simplify.
- [ ] **The respect check:** Would a person who just lost their home in a storm feel respected by this copy? Not
  pandered to, not ignored — respected.

If any check fails, rewrite before publishing. No exceptions.
