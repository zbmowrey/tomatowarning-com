# Tomato Warning: Storytelling & Content Strategy

## Document Purpose

This document defines how Tomato Warning creates, publishes, and governs content across all channels. It covers what we
say, where we say it, when we say it, and -- critically -- what we never say and when we stay silent. For a brand built
on storm metaphors that donates to disaster relief, content governance is not a nice-to-have. It is a survival
requirement.

---

## 1. Content Philosophy

**Build an audience, not just awareness.**

Awareness is someone seeing our jar on a shelf. An audience is someone who follows us, trusts us, and tells a friend. We
earn attention through content that is genuinely useful, entertaining, or meaningful -- not through paid interruption
alone.

**Three principles:**

1. **Flavor first, mission always.** Lead with what people came for (great salsa, interesting heat science, recipes
   worth making). Let the mission earn its place through credibility, not repetition.
2. **Earn the right to be playful.** Our storm theming is fun. It is also the language of real human tragedy. We earn
   the right to use it playfully by being the first to show up seriously when storms actually hit. Playfulness without
   credibility is tone-deafness.
3. **Prove everything.** Every claim we publish -- about ingredients, heat levels, charitable impact, small-batch
   production -- must be substantiated. If we cannot prove it, we do not publish it. This is not optional caution; it is
   brand insurance.

## 2. Content Pillars

Six thematic pillars organize all Tomato Warning content. Every piece of content maps to at least one pillar. No content
is published that falls outside these pillars.

### Pillar 1: Storm Culture

Weather science meets food culture. This is our most distinctive content lane -- no other salsa brand can own it.

- How the EF scale works (real meteorology, simplified)
- Storm season primers (hurricane prep, tornado safety, severe weather awareness)
- Weather phenomena explainers (supercells, squall lines, mesocyclones)
- Regional storm culture (Gulf Coast hurricane traditions, Tornado Alley life)
- Intersection of weather and food (cooking during power outages, shelf-stable meal prep)

**Guardrail:** Storm culture content is educational and respectful. It never trivializes severe weather or treats real
meteorological events as marketing opportunities. See Section 9: Content Guardrails.

### Pillar 2: Flavor Education

Heat science, food pairings, and cooking knowledge that positions Tomato Warning as a credible voice in the specialty
food space.

- Scoville scale explainers (what the numbers actually mean)
- Pepper profiles (habanero vs. ghost pepper vs. Carolina Reaper -- flavor, not just heat)
- Heat tolerance science (capsaicin receptors, building tolerance, the endorphin response)
- Ingredient deep dives (why black garlic, what makes a Florida tomato different)
- Pairing guides (each EF level matched to specific dishes, proteins, and occasions)

**Guardrail:** All heat and ingredient claims must be verifiable. Do not publish Scoville numbers, origin claims, or
health-adjacent statements without sourced evidence.

### Pillar 3: Impact Stories

How the brand's charitable mission comes to life. This is the most sensitive pillar. Done well, it builds lasting trust.
Done poorly, it becomes "purpose washing."

- Quarterly giving reports (specific dollar amounts, named recipient organizations)
- Partner organization spotlights (who they are, what they do, how our contribution was used)
- Disaster response coverage (what happened, who responded, how donations helped)
- Annual impact summaries (cumulative giving, communities reached, tangible outcomes)
- Fundraising partner stories (schools, clubs, and organizations that used our product)

**Guardrail:** Impact content must be factual, specific, and current. No vague claims ("we help communities"). No
emotional manipulation of disaster imagery. No impact stories published without verification from the recipient
organization. See Section 7: Impact Reporting Content for full requirements.

### Pillar 4: Behind the Scenes

Production transparency, ingredient sourcing, and the founder's genuine journey. Authenticity content that builds trust.

- Batch production process (what "small-batch" actually means for us -- define the number)
- Ingredient sourcing stories (where peppers come from, grower relationships, seasonal sourcing)
- Product development (recipe iteration, heat testing, flavor balancing)
- Founder's story (the Max origin story, the move to Florida, building the brand)
- Business building (honest reflections on starting a food brand -- challenges included)

**Guardrail:** "Small-batch" must be defined with a specific number (e.g., "batches of X jars"). "Florida tomatoes" must
come from Florida. Every sourcing claim must be traceable. Behind-the-scenes content is only authentic if it is true.

### Pillar 5: Community

Customer stories, user-generated content, and the people who make up the Tomato Warning audience.

- Customer spotlights (how people use the product, their favorite EF level, their recipes)
- User-generated content amplification (reposts, features, recipe shares)
- Recipe submissions and community challenges
- Retailer and partner features
- Fundraising success stories from organizations

**Guardrail:** All UGC must be used with explicit permission. Customer stories must be genuine (never fabricated or
incentivized to appear organic). See Section 6: User-Generated Content Strategy.

### Pillar 6: Seasonal & Cultural Moments

Content tied to the calendar -- weather seasons, food holidays, cultural moments, and retail events.

- Hurricane season awareness (June-November)
- Tornado season content (March-June)
- Food events (National Salsa Month - undeclared, Cinco de Mayo, Super Bowl, tailgating season)
- Holiday gifting guides (Thanksgiving, Christmas, Father's Day)
- Regional events (Gulf Coast food festivals, farmers market seasons)

**Guardrail:** Seasonal content during active storm seasons requires elevated review. See Section 8: Seasonal Content
Calendar and Section 9: Content Guardrails.

## 3. Content by Platform

Each platform serves a distinct purpose. Content is adapted to the platform, not copy-pasted across them.

### Instagram

**Purpose:** Visual storytelling and brand world-building.
**Content mix:** 50% product/food photography, 25% behind-the-scenes, 15% community/UGC, 10% impact.
**Format:** High-quality static images, Reels (15-60 sec), carousel educational posts, Stories for real-time/ephemeral
content.
**Tone:** Visually premium, concise copy, warm but confident.
**Posting cadence:** 3-5 feed posts/week, daily Stories during active periods.
**What works here:** The EF color gradient as a visual system. Pairing photography. Ingredient close-ups. Short recipe
videos. Customer unboxing.
**What does not work:** Long text posts. Hard sales pushes. Disaster imagery (even for mission content -- Instagram is
aspirational).

### TikTok

**Purpose:** Discovery, personality, and viral potential.
**Content mix:** 40% heat challenges/reactions, 30% behind-the-scenes/production, 20% recipe/food content, 10%
educational (weather/heat science).
**Format:** Short-form video (15-90 sec), trending audio, raw/authentic aesthetic.
**Tone:** Unpolished, genuine, playful. The founder's voice is the brand voice here.
**Posting cadence:** 3-5 videos/week.
**What works here:** EF-5 taste reactions. "Day in the life" production content. Recipe hacks. Storm fact overlays.
Duets with customers.
**What does not work:** Over-produced content. Corporate messaging. Anything that feels like an ad.
**Risk note:** EF-5 taste test content has viral potential but also liability risk. Never encourage unsafe consumption.
Always include a visible warning. Never feature minors tasting EF-4 or EF-5.

### X (Twitter)

**Purpose:** Brand personality, real-time conversation, and industry engagement.
**Content mix:** 40% witty brand voice, 30% engagement/replies, 20% links to long-form content, 10% real-time
commentary.
**Tone:** Sharp, witty, confident. The most "personality-forward" platform.
**Posting cadence:** 1-3 posts/day, active engagement throughout.
**What works here:** Storm/weather puns and wordplay. Quick takes on food trends. Engaging with food media and hot sauce
community. Real-time moments.
**What does not work:** Lengthy threads (save depth for blog/email). Automated posting without monitoring (especially
during weather events -- see Section 9).
**Critical risk:** X is the platform where brand missteps go viral fastest. ALL scheduled posts must be manually
reviewed during severe weather watches. Automated queues must have kill-switch capability.

### Email

**Purpose:** Depth, loyalty, and direct relationship.
**Content mix:** 30% product/new release announcements, 25% recipes and pairings, 20% impact reporting, 15%
behind-the-scenes, 10% promotions.
**Format:** Well-designed HTML emails, plain text for personal founder updates.
**Tone:** Warmer, more personal than social. The founder speaking directly.
**Sending cadence:** 2x/month for general list, 1x/month for minimal-engagement subscribers.
**What works here:** Quarterly impact reports (this is where Jennifer the Conscious Buyer lives). Exclusive recipes.
First access to new products. Honest founder reflections.
**What does not work:** High-frequency promotional blasts. Generic food newsletter content. Any promotional email during
an active weather emergency (see Section 9).

### Blog / Website

**Purpose:** SEO, depth, and evergreen content that drives organic discovery.
**Content mix:** 35% recipes with Tomato Warning products, 25% heat/flavor education, 20% impact reports and mission
content, 20% brand story and behind-the-scenes.
**Format:** Long-form articles (800-2,000 words), recipe posts with structured data, impact report pages.
**Tone:** Authoritative, informative, genuine.
**Publishing cadence:** 2-4 posts/month.
**What works here:** "The Science of Scoville" type educational content. Recipe content optimized for search. Annual
impact reports as permanent, linkable pages. Ingredient sourcing stories.
**What does not work:** Thin content published only for SEO. Content without genuine expertise or unique perspective.

## 4. Narrative Arc Over Time

The brand story evolves across four phases. Content strategy shifts with each phase.

### Phase 1: Pre-Launch (Current)

**Narrative:** "Something is forming on the radar."
**Content focus:** Mystery, anticipation, origin story teases.

- Email signup collection with minimal reveal
- Founder story hints on social media
- Brand identity preview (colors, EF scale concept, storm aesthetic)
- Zero product claims until products are finalized and verified

**Tone:** Intriguing, restrained, confident.
**Risk:** Overpromising before the product exists. Keep claims aspirational but honest.

### Phase 2: Launch (Months 1-6)

**Narrative:** "The storm has arrived."
**Content focus:** Product introduction, origin story in full, first sales, early reviews.

- Full product lineup reveal with verified claims only
- Origin story published across all platforms
- Recipe content establishing each EF level as a cooking companion
- First farmers market and DTC experiences documented
- Initial charitable commitment announced with SPECIFIC details (percentage, named partner, reporting schedule)

**Tone:** Energetic, proud, inviting.
**Risk:** The temptation to overstate early traction. Report honestly. "We sold 200 jars at our first market" is more
credible than "overwhelming demand."

### Phase 3: Establishment (Months 6-18)

**Narrative:** "Tracking a path forward."
**Content focus:** Customer proof, retail expansion, first impact report, earned media.

- Customer testimonials and UGC featured prominently
- Retail partner announcements (with context on what this means for the brand)
- FIRST QUARTERLY IMPACT REPORT -- this is the credibility inflection point
- Deeper recipe content and food pairing guides
- Community building through challenges and engagement

**Tone:** Credible, community-driven, steady.
**Risk:** Failing to publish the first impact report on time. If we promise quarterly reports and miss the first one,
the charitable mission loses credibility instantly.

### Phase 4: Growth (18+ Months)

**Narrative:** "A system that sustains."
**Content focus:** Scale story, deeper impact, category authority, community leadership.

- Annual impact summary with cumulative giving
- Category expertise content (becoming a voice in premium salsa / hot sauce)
- Expansion stories told honestly (new markets, new retail, new products)
- Community spotlights as primary social proof
- Potential brand collaborations and partnerships

**Tone:** Established, generous, authoritative.
**Risk:** Losing the scrappy authenticity as the brand grows. Content should resist the pull toward corporate polish.

## 5. Recipe Content Framework

Recipes are the highest-utility content we can produce. Every EF level becomes a cooking companion, not just a heat
level.

### Structure for Recipe Content

Each recipe post follows this format:

1. **Dish name and EF pairing** (e.g., "Grilled Mahi Tacos with EF-3 Squall Line")
2. **Why this pairing works** (flavor science: why habanero-mango complements grilled fish)
3. **Full recipe with clear instructions** (tested, photographed, reproducible)
4. **Heat customization note** (how to adjust up or down on the EF scale)
5. **Serving suggestion and occasion** (weeknight dinner, game day, date night)

### Recipe Content by EF Level

| EF Level          | Cooking Position                    | Recipe Occasions                            |
|-------------------|-------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| EF-1 Coastal Calm | Everyday condiment, family-friendly | Weeknight dinners, kids' meals, brunch      |
| EF-2 Gulf Breeze  | Versatile cooking ingredient        | Grilling, slow cooking, marinades           |
| EF-3 Squall Line  | Bold flavor accent                  | Tacos, bowls, tropical dishes               |
| EF-4 Supercell    | Serious heat for experienced cooks  | Steaks, ribs, chili, craft cocktails        |
| EF-5 Ground Zero  | Challenge/experience product        | Wings, dares, Bloody Marys, bragging rights |

### Recipe SEO Strategy

- Target long-tail keywords: "habanero mango fish tacos recipe," "ghost pepper steak sauce"
- Include structured data (schema.org Recipe markup) for Google rich results
- Publish 1-2 recipe posts per month minimum
- Cross-reference EF levels in every recipe to build internal linking

## 6. User-Generated Content Strategy

UGC is the most credible content a brand can publish. It is also the most legally and ethically sensitive.

### How We Inspire UGC

- **The EF Challenge:** Encourage customers to share their EF level journey (starting at EF-1, working up). Hashtag:
  #MyEFLevel
- **Recipe sharing:** Invite customers to share dishes made with Tomato Warning. Feature the best on brand channels.
- **Unboxing and first-taste moments:** Encourage video reactions, especially for gift set recipients.
- **Storm stories:** Invite customers to share their own storm experiences (carefully -- see guardrails below).

### How We Collect UGC

- Branded hashtag monitoring (#TomatoWarning, #MyEFLevel, #ChaseTheStorm)
- Post-purchase email prompts (7-14 days after delivery)
- Social media engagement and reposts
- Quarterly community challenges with small prizes (product, not cash)

### How We Amplify UGC

- Repost on Instagram Stories and feed (with credit and permission)
- Feature in email newsletters (customer spotlight section)
- Compile on website testimonials page
- Use in paid social ads (with explicit written consent)

### UGC Guardrails

- **Permission is mandatory.** No UGC is reposted or used without explicit permission from the creator.
- **No fabricated UGC.** Every customer story must be real. Paying someone to pretend to be an organic customer is
  fraud.
- **No storm trauma content.** If a customer shares a disaster story alongside our product, we do not amplify it as
  marketing content. Their experience is not our advertisement.
- **No minors consuming EF-4 or EF-5.** If UGC shows a minor eating high-heat products, we do not share it regardless of
  context.
- **FTC disclosure compliance.** If any UGC creator received free product, it must be disclosed per FTC guidelines.

## 7. Impact Reporting Content

This is the most important content we will ever publish. The charitable mission is either our greatest asset or our
greatest liability. Impact reporting is what determines which one it becomes.

### Reporting Cadence

| Report Type                | Frequency      | Format                    | Channel                                |
|----------------------------|----------------|---------------------------|----------------------------------------|
| Quarterly impact update    | Every 3 months | Blog post + email         | Website, email, social summary         |
| Annual impact summary      | Yearly         | Comprehensive report page | Website (permanent), email, social, PR |
| Specific event response    | As needed      | Blog post + social        | Website, email, social                 |
| Fundraising partner report | Semi-annually  | Blog post                 | Website, email to partners             |

### What Every Impact Report Must Include

1. **Specific dollar amount donated** (not "a portion" -- the actual number)
2. **Named recipient organization(s)** (not "disaster relief charities" -- the actual names)
3. **What the money was used for** (not "helping communities" -- specific: meals served, supplies delivered, families
   housed)
4. **Time period covered** (exact dates)
5. **How the number was calculated** (percentage of net profits for the period, with enough context to be verifiable)

### What Impact Reports Must Never Include

- Disaster imagery used for emotional manipulation
- Inflated or rounded-up numbers
- Vague claims without specifics ("we've helped thousands")
- Language that takes credit for the relief organization's work (we funded it; they did it)
- Charitable impact tied to individual jar purchases (e.g., "your jar provided 3 meals") unless mathematically
  verifiable

### The Pre-Profit Problem

In Year 1, net profits may be zero or negative. This creates a content problem: how do we talk about the charitable
mission before there are profits to donate?

**Approved approaches:**

- "Our mission is to donate at least 10% of net profits to disaster relief. As a new brand, we are building toward that
  commitment and will publish our first impact report in [specific quarter]."
- Highlight the fundraising partner channel (products at/below cost to school groups), which operates independent of
  profits.
- Document the process of selecting charitable partners (shows commitment is structural, not performative).

**Prohibited approaches:**

- "Every jar helps disaster relief" (not true until profitable)
- "A portion of your purchase goes to charity" (misleading if no profits yet)
- Any language that implies current, active giving before giving has actually occurred

## 9. Content Guardrails

**This is the most important section in this document.** Every other section tells us what to create. This section tells
us what not to create, when to stop creating, and how to respond when content creates a problem.

### 9.1 Active Weather Emergency Protocol

**Trigger:** National Weather Service issues a Watch or Warning for any category of severe weather (tornado, hurricane,
severe thunderstorm, tropical storm) in any geographic area where Tomato Warning has customers, retail presence, or
brand awareness.

**Immediate actions (within 1 hour of Watch/Warning):**

1. **PAUSE all scheduled social media posts.** Every platform. No exceptions. One person (designated Content Lead) has
   authority and responsibility to execute the pause.
2. **PAUSE all scheduled promotional emails.** Any email in queue that contains product promotion, playful storm
   language, or could be perceived as insensitive is held.
3. **REVIEW all recently published content** (last 48 hours) for anything that could be screenshot'd alongside disaster
   coverage and look tone-deaf. Hide or archive if necessary.
4. **ACTIVATE the pivot protocol** (see 9.2).

**Duration:** The pause remains in effect until 48 hours AFTER the event has concluded AND initial damage/casualty
assessments are complete. We are not the first voice back. We wait.

**Authority:** The Content Lead (or CEO in their absence) is the sole decision-maker for pause and resume. This is not a
committee decision during a crisis.

### 9.2 Pivot Messaging During Weather Events

When an active weather event is affecting communities, our content shifts entirely.

**What we publish:**

- Safety information and emergency resources (NWS links, FEMA resources, local shelter info)
- Signal boosts for verified relief organizations (especially our named partners)
- A simple, sincere statement: "Our thoughts are with everyone affected by [specific event]. Here's how to
  help: [specific links]."
- Updates on our own response (if we are making an emergency donation, say so with specifics)

**What we never publish during an active event:**

- ANY product promotion, regardless of how subtle
- ANY playful storm language, puns, or EF-scale references
- ANY content that could be perceived as leveraging the disaster for brand awareness
- ANY disaster imagery for marketing purposes
- ANY "thoughts and prayers" without accompanying action or resource links

**Tone during events:** Direct, helpful, humble. We are a salsa company. We are not first responders. We do not center
ourselves in the disaster narrative. We share resources, we donate, and we stay quiet otherwise.

### 9.3 The Screenshot Test

Before any content is published, it must pass this test:

> If this single piece of content were screenshot'd, removed from all context, and shared by someone hostile to the
> brand, would it make us look bad?

For most brands, this is a nice-to-have quality check. For a storm-themed brand that donates to disaster relief, it is
existential. Every piece of content with storm language, destruction metaphors, or charitable claims must pass this
test.

**Common screenshot risks:**

- Playful storm language published the same week as a deadly weather event
- Charitable claims that are vague or unverifiable
- EF-5 "dare" content that could be framed as encouraging dangerous consumption
- Product stories with destruction/devastation language taken out of their product context
- Any content that juxtaposes "fun storm branding" with "serious disaster relief"

### 9.4 Sensitivity Categories

Content involving these topics requires elevated review (minimum two reviewers, one of whom must be outside the content
team):

| Category                    | Why It's Sensitive                                 | Review Requirement                             |
|-----------------------------|----------------------------------------------------|------------------------------------------------|
| Active weather events       | Our brand theme is someone's tragedy               | Full pause + pivot protocol                    |
| Disaster relief claims      | FTC scrutiny, consumer trust, purpose washing risk | Specific, verifiable, approved language only   |
| EF-5 / extreme heat content | Physical safety, liability, "dare culture"         | Safety warnings mandatory, no minors           |
| Storm/destruction metaphors | Dual meaning (fun branding vs. real tragedy)       | Screenshot test required                       |
| Origin story (Max)          | Involves a minor, personal family story            | Founder approval required, no exploitation     |
| "Ground Zero" naming        | 9/11 association                                   | See note below                                 |
| Competitor references       | Legal risk, brand positioning                      | No direct competitor disparagement             |
| Health claims               | FDA/FTC regulation                                 | No health claims whatsoever unless FDA-cleared |

**Note on "Ground Zero":** The EF-5 product name carries significant risk due to its association with the September 11
attacks. Content featuring this product by name should be reviewed for sensitivity. The content team recommends the
brand council evaluate whether a name change would reduce unnecessary risk. If the name is retained, we must have a
pre-prepared, empathetic response for when the association is raised publicly -- because it will be.

### 9.5 Charitable Claim Compliance

All content mentioning the charitable mission must comply with these rules:

**Required:**

- Specific percentage cited ("at least 10% of net profits")
- Named recipient organization(s)
- Time-bound commitment ("annually," "quarterly")
- Clear distinction between aspirational and actual giving (pre-profit vs. post-profit)

**Prohibited:**

- "Every purchase helps" (unverifiable on a per-purchase basis)
- "Portion of proceeds" (vague, FTC red flag)
- Dollar-per-jar calculations unless mathematically verified and current
- Implying current giving before first impact report is published
- Using disaster imagery to drive product sales
- Any claim that positions buying salsa as equivalent to donating to charity

**Approved phrasing examples:**

- "Tomato Warning targets at least 10% of net profits to disaster relief organizations, reported quarterly."
- "We partner with [Named Organization] to support disaster recovery in affected communities."
- "See our latest impact report at [link] for specific giving details."

### 9.6 Content Crisis Response Playbook

If content causes a negative public reaction:

**Tier 1 -- Minor (isolated negative comments, small-scale criticism):**

- Acknowledge and respond respectfully if the criticism is valid
- Do not delete negative comments unless they violate community guidelines (profanity, threats, harassment)
- Document the concern for future content planning
- No public statement needed

**Tier 2 -- Moderate (content picked up by media or influencers, gaining traction):**

- Immediate review by Content Lead and CEO
- Prepare a public response within 4 hours
- Response must be specific, sincere, and non-defensive
- If the criticism is valid, acknowledge it explicitly: "You're right. We made a mistake. Here's what we're doing about
  it."
- Pause all non-essential content until the situation is assessed

**Tier 3 -- Severe (viral negative coverage, brand reputation at risk):**

- All content paused immediately across all platforms
- CEO leads response with support from the full team
- External PR counsel if available
- Public response within 2 hours acknowledging the issue
- Specific corrective actions announced (not just "we'll do better")
- Post-crisis review within one week to identify systemic failures

**In all tiers:**

- Never delete the original content after it has been screenshot'd (deletion looks like cover-up)
- Never blame the audience for misunderstanding
- Never issue a non-apology ("we're sorry if anyone was offended")
- Respond with specifics, not platitudes

## 10. Content Measurement

Engagement metrics (likes, shares, open rates) are vanity metrics unless connected to business outcomes. We measure
content on what it actually drives.

### Primary Metrics (Business Impact)

| Metric                                                    | What It Tells Us                        | Target                                      |
|-----------------------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------|---------------------------------------------|
| Email list growth rate                                    | Content is attracting interested people | 10%+ month-over-month in Year 1             |
| Email-to-purchase conversion                              | Content drives actual sales             | 2-5% of list purchasing per quarter         |
| Website traffic from organic search                       | SEO content is working                  | 500+ organic sessions/month by Month 6      |
| Recipe page engagement (time on page, print/save actions) | Recipe content has utility              | 3+ min avg time on page                     |
| Impact report page views and shares                       | Mission content resonates               | Every report viewed by 25%+ of email list   |
| Referral traffic from social to website                   | Social converts to owned audience       | 15%+ of social followers visit site monthly |

### Secondary Metrics (Content Health)

| Metric                              | What It Tells Us                         | Watch For                                                                                 |
|-------------------------------------|------------------------------------------|-------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------|
| Social engagement rate              | Content resonates with existing audience | Declining rates suggest content fatigue                                                   |
| UGC submission volume               | Community is actively engaged            | Track monthly; seasonal variation expected                                                |
| Email unsubscribe rate              | Content frequency or quality issue       | Above 1% per send requires review                                                         |
| Negative sentiment mentions         | Brand perception risk                    | ANY mention of insensitivity, purpose washing, or tone-deafness triggers immediate review |
| Content-attributed customer surveys | Why people buy and stay                  | Quarterly: "What content do you value from us?"                                           |

### Measurement Discipline

- **Monthly:** Review primary and secondary metrics, adjust content mix if needed.
- **Quarterly:** Deep analysis tied to impact report cadence. Correlate content output with sales, list growth, and
  retention.
- **Annually:** Full content audit -- what worked, what didn't, what was risky, what was missed.
- **After every weather event:** Review content performance and crisis response effectiveness. Document lessons learned.

### What We Do Not Measure (And Why)

- **Follower count as a primary metric.** Followers without engagement or conversion are vanity.
- **Virality for its own sake.** A viral post that damages brand trust is a net negative regardless of reach.
- **Content volume.** Publishing more is not inherently better. Publishing fewer, higher-quality pieces that drive
  business outcomes is the goal.

This content strategy does not exist in isolation. It depends on and must align with:

|----------------------------------------------|-----------------------------------------------------------------|

disaster relief" but the sales playbook includes that language, we have a problem. The Brand Council should
cross-reference all seven documents before finalizing any of them.
